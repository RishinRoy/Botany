Evidences, Theories and Mechanism of Evolution

Evolution is the gradual change in living organisms over generations. It explains how present-day organisms have arisen from earlier forms and how diversity, adaptation and new species are produced. Evolution includes two related aspects: the origin of life and the subsequent evolution of living organisms. The origin of life has been explained by several theories such as special creation, spontaneous generation, biogenesis, panspermia and chemical evolution. The fact that organisms have changed over time is supported by evidences from fossils, comparative morphology, embryology, biogeography, molecular biology and cytology. The mechanism of evolution includes variation, inheritance, natural selection, genetic drift, gene flow, isolation and speciation.

Evidence

Evidences of Evolution

1. Fossil Evidence

Fossils are the preserved remains, impressions or traces of ancient organisms found in sedimentary rocks. They provide direct historical evidence of organisms that lived in the past. Fossils show that many earlier organisms are now extinct and that present organisms are related to ancient forms.

The fossil record shows a gradual change from simpler to more complex forms through geological time. In plants, fossils such as Rhynia show simple early vascular plants with leafless and rootless axes. Later fossil plants show the development of leaves, roots, seeds and finally flowers. Fossils of pteridophytes, seed ferns, gymnosperms and early angiosperms help us understand major steps in plant evolution.

Fossils are important because they provide a time sequence of life forms and support the idea that life has changed gradually over millions of years.

2. Comparative Morphology and Anatomy

Comparative morphology and anatomy provide evidence of evolution by comparing the external and internal structures of organisms. When different organisms show the same basic structural plan, it indicates that they may have evolved from a common ancestor.

Homologous organs

Homologous organs have the same origin and basic structure but perform different functions. They show divergent evolution, where one ancestral structure becomes modified for different functions.

Example: Leaf tendril of pea, leaf spine of cactus and normal foliage leaf are all modifications of leaves. Their functions are different, but their origin is the same.

Analogous organs

Analogous organs perform similar functions but have different origins. They show convergent evolution, where unrelated structures become similar due to similar functions.

Example: Leaf tendril of pea and stem tendril of Cucurbita both help in climbing, but one originates from leaf and the other from stem.

Thus, comparative morphology shows both common ancestry and adaptive modification.

3. Vestigial and Reduced Structures

Vestigial organs are reduced structures that were functional in ancestors but are reduced or less functional in present organisms. Their presence indicates evolutionary history.

In plants, reduced leaves in some xerophytes, sterile stamens called staminodes, and reduced floral organs in some flowers can be considered examples of reduction. These structures suggest that present-day plants still retain traces of ancestral structures.

Vestigial structures support evolution because they cannot be properly explained unless we accept that organisms have changed from earlier forms.

4. Embryological Evidence

Embryology deals with the development of organisms from the zygote. Related organisms often show similarities in their early developmental stages, even though their adult structures may differ.

In angiosperms, many developmental processes are similar across different groups. These include embryo sac formation, fertilization, zygote formation, proembryo stage, suspensor formation and differentiation of embryo into root and shoot regions. The common occurrence of similar developmental stages suggests common ancestry.

Embryological evidence is important because development often preserves traces of evolutionary relationships.

5. Biogeographical Evidence

Biogeography is the study of the geographical distribution of organisms. The distribution of plants and animals across the world supports evolution.

Closely related species are often found in nearby regions, while isolated regions such as islands may have unique endemic species. These species evolve separately because of geographical isolation. For example, island floras often contain species that are not found elsewhere.

Biogeography shows that isolation, migration and environmental conditions play important roles in evolution and speciation.

6. Molecular and Biochemical Evidence

Molecular evidence is one of the strongest modern evidences of evolution. All living organisms contain similar biomolecules such as DNA, RNA, proteins, carbohydrates and lipids. They also use ATP as an energy molecule and share a nearly universal genetic code.

Closely related organisms show greater similarity in DNA sequences, proteins and enzymes. Less related organisms show more differences. This pattern supports the idea that organisms have descended from common ancestors.

The biochemical unity of life strongly indicates that all living organisms originated from a common primitive life form.

7. Cytological Evidence

Cytology also supports evolution. Similarities in cell structure, chromosomes, mitosis and meiosis indicate relationships among organisms.

In plants, chromosome number and chromosome structure are important in evolution. Changes such as duplication, deletion, translocation and polyploidy create variation. Polyploidy, or increase in chromosome sets, is especially important in plant evolution. Many angiosperms and crop plants have evolved through polyploidy.

Thus, cytological evidence shows that genetic and chromosomal changes contribute to evolution.

Origin of life

Theories of Origin of Life

1. Theory of Special Creation

The theory of special creation states that life was created by a supernatural power or God. According to this view, all organisms were created in their present form and have remained unchanged.

This theory was widely accepted in ancient times because scientific knowledge about fossils, cells, heredity and evolution was not available. It gives a religious or philosophical explanation of life.

However, special creation is not considered a scientific theory because it cannot be tested by experiments or observations. Science explains natural events through natural causes, while this theory depends on supernatural explanation.

2. Theory of Spontaneous Generation or Abiogenesis

The old theory of spontaneous generation stated that life could arise suddenly from non-living or decaying matter. It was believed that maggots developed from rotten meat, frogs arose from mud and microorganisms appeared from dirty water.

This idea was later disproved by experiments. Redi showed that maggots arise from eggs laid by flies and not from meat itself. Spallanzani showed that boiled and sealed broth did not produce microorganisms. Pasteur finally disproved spontaneous generation through his swan-neck flask experiment. He showed that microorganisms appear in broth only when microbes from air enter it.

Thus, spontaneous generation was rejected because life does not arise suddenly from non-living matter under present conditions.

3. Theory of Biogenesis

The theory of biogenesis states that life arises only from pre-existing life. This theory was strongly supported by Pasteur’s experiment.

According to biogenesis, microorganisms do not appear automatically in sterile material. They develop only when already existing microorganisms enter and multiply.

Biogenesis is accepted for present-day conditions. All living organisms today arise from pre-existing living organisms. However, biogenesis does not explain how the first life originated on primitive Earth. Therefore, it explains the continuity of life, but not the first origin of life.

4. Panspermia or Cosmozoic Theory

The panspermia theory, also called the cosmozoic theory, states that life did not originate on Earth but came from outer space. According to this theory, spores, microorganisms or organic particles reached Earth through meteorites, comets or cosmic dust.

This theory is supported to some extent by the discovery of organic compounds in meteorites. It also suggests that the building blocks of life may exist in space.

However, panspermia does not explain the actual origin of life. It only shifts the problem from Earth to another place in the universe. It also has difficulty explaining how living spores could survive extreme cold, radiation and heat during space travel. Therefore, panspermia is interesting but incomplete as a theory of origin of life.

5. Chemical Evolution Theory of Oparin and Haldane

The chemical evolution theory was proposed independently by A. I. Oparin and J. B. S. Haldane. It is the most accepted scientific theory for the origin of life.

According to this theory, life originated gradually from non-living matter through chemical reactions on primitive Earth. The primitive atmosphere was reducing and contained gases such as methane, ammonia, hydrogen and water vapour. Energy from lightning, ultraviolet rays and volcanic heat caused these gases to form simple organic molecules. These molecules accumulated in primitive oceans, forming a “hot dilute soup” or “prebiotic soup”.

Later, simple organic molecules formed complex molecules, which aggregated into coacervates or protocells. Finally, the first living cells evolved. The Miller-Urey experiment supported this theory by producing amino acids under simulated primitive Earth conditions.

Thus, chemical evolution explains the origin of first life through gradual chemical changes.

Mechanism

Mechanism of Evolution

The mechanism of evolution explains how populations change over generations and how new species arise. It includes the following processes.

1. Variation

Variation means differences among individuals of the same species. It is the raw material of evolution. Without variation, evolution cannot occur. Variations are due to mutation, recombination, crossing over, independent assortment and random fertilization. In plants, hybridization and polyploidy also produce important variations.

2. Mutation

Mutation is a sudden heritable change in genetic material. It may produce new alleles and new characters. Some mutations are harmful, some are neutral, and some may be useful. Useful mutations may help organisms survive better in a particular environment and may become common in a population over generations.

3. Recombination

Recombination occurs during sexual reproduction. Crossing over and independent assortment during meiosis produce new combinations of genes. Random fertilization also adds genetic variation. In plants, cross-pollination is important because it brings together genes from different individuals and increases variation.

4. Natural Selection

Natural selection is the process by which organisms with favourable variations survive and reproduce better than others. Unfavourable variations are gradually eliminated. For example, in a dry habitat, plants with deeper roots or better drought resistance may survive better. Over generations, such favourable traits become more common in the population. Natural selection does not create variation; it acts on existing variation.

5. Genetic Drift

Genetic drift is a random change in gene frequency in a population. It is especially important in small populations. Due to chance events, some genes may become common while others may disappear. Genetic drift can cause isolated populations to become different from the original population, helping in speciation.

6. Gene Flow

Gene flow is the movement of genes from one population to another. In plants, it may occur through pollen transfer, seed dispersal or hybridization. Gene flow increases variation within a population, but it can also reduce differences between populations if interbreeding continues.

7. Isolation

Isolation prevents interbreeding between populations. It may be geographical, ecological, seasonal, mechanical or genetic. When populations remain isolated for a long time, they accumulate different variations. Over time, these differences may become so great that the populations can no longer interbreed.

8. Speciation

Speciation is the formation of new species. It occurs when populations become genetically different and reproductively isolated. In plants, speciation may occur due to geographical isolation, hybridization and polyploidy. Polyploidy is especially important because chromosome doubling can quickly produce a new plant species.

Summary of mechanism: Variation → inheritance → natural selection / genetic drift → adaptation → isolation → speciation
ConclusionEvolution is supported by evidences from fossils, comparative morphology, vestigial structures, embryology, biogeography, molecular biology and cytology. Theories of origin of life include special creation, spontaneous generation, biogenesis, panspermia and chemical evolution. Among these, chemical evolution proposed by Oparin and Haldane is the most accepted scientific explanation for the origin of first life. Evolution then proceeds through mechanisms such as variation, mutation, recombination, natural selection, genetic drift, gene flow, isolation and speciation. Thus, evolution explains both the origin of life and the diversity of living organisms.

Hardy-Weinberg Principle

Gene Frequency, Genotypic Frequency and Genetic Drift

The Hardy-Weinberg principle is an important principle of population genetics. It was independently proposed by G. H. Hardy and W. Weinberg in 1908. This principle explains the relationship between gene frequency and genotypic frequency in a population. According to this principle, in a large randomly mating population, the gene and genotype frequencies remain constant from generation to generation, provided there is no mutation, migration, natural selection, genetic drift or non-random mating. Such a population is said to be in Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium.

Principle

Statement of Hardy-Weinberg Principle

StatementIn the absence of evolutionary forces, the relative frequencies of alleles and genotypes in a sexually reproducing population remain constant from generation to generation.

This means that if no external force acts on a population, the population does not evolve. Evolution occurs only when Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium is disturbed.

Gene Frequency

Gene frequency or allele frequency is the proportion of a particular allele in the gene pool of a population.

For a gene with two alleles, A and a:

  • Frequency of allele A is represented by p.
  • Frequency of allele a is represented by q.
p + q = 1

This means the total frequency of all alleles in the population is always 1.

Example: If the frequency of allele A is 0.7, then p = 0.7. Since p + q = 1, q = 1 − 0.7 = 0.3. So, the frequency of allele a is 0.3.

Genotypic Frequency

Genotypic frequency is the proportion of a particular genotype in a population.

For two alleles, A and a, there are three possible genotypes:

  1. AA — homozygous dominant
  2. Aa — heterozygous
  3. aa — homozygous recessive
p2 + 2pq + q2 = 1

This equation is the Hardy-Weinberg equation.

GenotypeFrequencyMeaning

Explanation of Hardy-Weinberg Equation

If the frequency of allele A is p and the frequency of allele a is q, then during random mating:

  • The chance of A combining with A is p × p = p2.
  • The chance of A combining with a is p × q.
  • The chance of a combining with A is q × p.
  • The total heterozygous frequency is pq + qp = 2pq.
  • The chance of a combining with a is q × q = q2.

Thus, AA = p2, Aa = 2pq and aa = q2. Therefore, the Hardy-Weinberg equation is:

p2 + 2pq + q2 = 1

Equilibrium

Conditions for Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium

1. Large Population Size

The population must be very large. In small populations, chance events can change gene frequencies.

2. Random Mating

All individuals must have equal chance of mating. If mating is selective or non-random, genotype frequencies change.

3. No Mutation

There should be no mutation converting one allele into another.

4. No Migration

There should be no movement of individuals into or out of the population. Migration can introduce or remove alleles.

5. No Natural Selection

All genotypes must have equal survival and reproductive success. If one genotype survives better, gene frequencies will change.

6. No Genetic Drift

Gene frequencies should not change by chance, especially in small populations.

If any of these conditions are disturbed, the population may evolve.

Genetic drift

Genetic Drift and Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium

Definition

Genetic drift is the random change in gene frequency in a population due to chance events. It is not caused by natural selection. It is especially important in small populations.

In genetic drift, some alleles may become common or disappear purely by chance, even if they are not harmful or beneficial.

How Genetic Drift Affects Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium

The Hardy-Weinberg principle assumes a very large population. But in small populations, random events may change allele frequencies. This disturbs Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium and causes evolution.

For example, if a few individuals of a plant population are destroyed by flood, fire or disease, some alleles may be lost by chance. The surviving population may have a different gene frequency from the original population.

Thus, genetic drift changes gene frequency randomly and may lead to evolutionary change.

1. Bottleneck Effect

The bottleneck effect occurs when a large population is suddenly reduced to a small size due to natural calamities such as flood, fire, drought, disease or human activity.

The few surviving individuals may not represent the original gene pool. As a result, some alleles may be lost and gene frequency changes.

Example: If only a few plants survive after a forest fire, the next generation will arise from those survivors only. Their allele frequencies may be different from the original population.

2. Founder Effect

The founder effect occurs when a few individuals from a large population migrate and establish a new population in a new area.

The new population contains only the alleles carried by the founders. Therefore, its gene frequency may differ from the original population.

Example: If a few seeds of a plant species are carried to an isolated island and form a new population, the gene pool of that island population may be different from the original mainland population.

Genetic drift is one such evolutionary force, causing random changes in gene frequency, especially in small populations. Therefore, the Hardy-Weinberg principle helps us understand both genetic stability and evolutionary change in populations.

Microevolution, Macroevolution and Origin of Life

Evolution is the gradual change in living organisms over generations. It explains how simple ancestral forms gave rise to the diversity of organisms seen today. Evolution acts at two levels: microevolution and macroevolution. Microevolution deals with small genetic changes within populations, whereas macroevolution deals with large-scale evolutionary changes leading to the origin of new species and higher groups. A complete idea of evolution also includes the origin of life, which explains how life appeared on primitive Earth and how it gradually developed through geological time.

Concept of Evolution

Evolution is based on the idea that living organisms are not fixed and unchanging. Populations show variation, and these variations may be inherited. When certain variations help organisms survive better in a particular environment, they are preserved through natural selection. Over generations, useful variations accumulate and populations become better adapted.

Evolution explains both the unity of life and the diversity of life. Unity is shown by common features such as cellular organization, DNA, genetic code and metabolism. Diversity is produced by adaptation, isolation, speciation and long-term evolutionary change.

Thus, evolution connects origin of life, adaptation, biodiversity, speciation and the development of major plant groups.

Small-scale change

Microevolution

Definition

Microevolution refers to small-scale evolutionary changes occurring within a population or species. It mainly involves changes in gene frequency or allele frequency from one generation to the next.

It does not usually produce a major new group immediately, but it is the basic foundation of evolution.

Explanation

A population contains different alleles of genes. If the frequency of these alleles changes due to mutation, recombination, natural selection, genetic drift or gene flow, microevolution occurs.

For example, in a plant population, some individuals may tolerate drought better than others. If the environment becomes dry, drought-tolerant plants survive and reproduce more successfully. Over generations, genes for drought tolerance increase in the population. This is microevolution.

Thus, microevolution explains how populations gradually become adapted to their local environment.

Examples of Microevolution

Microevolution can be seen in the development of antibiotic resistance in bacteria, pesticide resistance in insects and herbicide resistance in weeds. In plants, the formation of local ecotypes adapted to dry, saline, cold or high-altitude habitats is also an example of microevolution.

For example, two populations of the same plant species growing in different habitats may slowly become different in height, leaf size, flowering time or stress tolerance. These are small evolutionary changes within the species.

Importance of Microevolution

Microevolution is important because it explains adaptation within populations. It shows how small genetic changes accumulate over generations. If such changes continue for a long time and populations become isolated, they may eventually form new species. Therefore, microevolution is the starting point of macroevolution.

Large-scale change

Macroevolution

Definition

Macroevolution refers to large-scale evolutionary changes that occur over long geological periods. It results in the origin of new species, genera, families, orders or major groups of organisms.

It includes major evolutionary events such as the origin of land plants, vascular tissues, seeds, flowers and fruits.

Explanation

Macroevolution operates above the species level. It is not just a minor change in gene frequency but involves major structural, reproductive and ecological changes. These changes usually take place over millions of years and are studied through fossils, comparative morphology, embryology and molecular evidence.

In plants, macroevolution can be seen in the transition from aquatic algal ancestors to land plants, from non-vascular plants to vascular plants, from homospory to heterospory, from naked seeds to flowers and fruits.

Thus, macroevolution explains the origin of major plant groups and large evolutionary trends.

The relationship can be shown as: Microevolution → population change → adaptation → isolation → speciation → macroevolution. Thus, macroevolution may be considered the large-scale result of accumulated microevolutionary changes over geological time.

Later, photosynthesis, oxygenation, eukaryotic cells, multicellularity, land plants, vascular plants, seed plants, gymnosperms and angiosperms evolved. Thus, evolution explains the origin, continuity, adaptation and diversity of life.

Lamarckism, Darwinism, Neo-Darwinism and Modern Synthetic Theory

Evolution is the gradual change in organisms over generations, leading to the formation of new forms and species from pre-existing ones. Several theories have been proposed to explain the process of evolution. Among them, Lamarckism, Darwinism, Neo-Darwinism and the Modern Synthetic Theory are important. Lamarckism explains evolution through inheritance of acquired characters. Darwinism explains evolution through natural selection. Neo-Darwinism modifies Darwin’s theory by emphasizing heritable variations and rejecting acquired characters. The Modern Synthetic Theory gives the most accepted explanation by combining Darwinism with genetics, mutation, recombination, population genetics and speciation.

Theory 1

Lamarckism

Meaning

Lamarckism was proposed by Jean Baptiste de Lamarck in his book Philosophie Zoologique in 1809. It is also called the theory of inheritance of acquired characters.

According to Lamarck, organisms change during their lifetime due to environmental influence, use and disuse of organs, and these acquired changes are transmitted to the next generation.

1. Internal Vital Force

Lamarck believed that every living organism has an internal force or tendency to increase in size, complexity and perfection. According to him, this internal vital force drives organisms from simpler forms towards more complex forms.

For example, Lamarck thought that simple organisms gradually became more complex because of this natural internal tendency.

This idea is not accepted today because evolution does not occur due to a mysterious internal force. However, it was important historically because Lamarck tried to explain progressive change in organisms.

2. Effect of Environment and New Needs

According to Lamarck, changes in the environment create new needs in organisms. When surroundings change, organisms must adjust themselves to survive. These new needs lead to new habits and changes in the use of organs.

For example, if food becomes scarce near the ground and is available only on tall trees, animals may try to stretch their necks to reach the leaves. Similarly, aquatic plants may develop features suited to water, while desert plants develop features suited to dry conditions.

Thus, Lamarck gave importance to the role of environment in producing adaptive changes.

3. Use and Disuse of Organs

This is one of the most important postulates of Lamarckism. Lamarck stated that organs which are used continuously become stronger, larger and better developed. On the other hand, organs which are not used become weak, reduced and may finally disappear.

  • The long neck of giraffe was explained as the result of repeated stretching to reach leaves of tall trees.
  • The strong limbs of running animals were explained by constant use.
  • The loss of limbs in snakes was explained by disuse.
  • The reduced eyes of cave animals were explained by disuse in darkness.

Thus, according to Lamarck, use strengthens an organ and disuse weakens it.

4. Inheritance of Acquired Characters

Lamarck believed that characters acquired by an organism during its lifetime are inherited by offspring. If an organ becomes stronger due to use, that improvement is passed to the next generation. Similarly, if an organ becomes reduced due to disuse, that reduction is also inherited.

Over many generations, these acquired changes accumulate and lead to the formation of new species.

For example, according to Lamarck, giraffes acquired longer necks by stretching. Their offspring inherited slightly longer necks. Repetition of this process for many generations produced modern long-necked giraffes.

Merits of Lamarckism

Lamarckism was one of the earliest scientific attempts to explain evolution. It rejected the idea that organisms are fixed and unchanging. Lamarck correctly emphasized that organisms change with time and that the environment has an important role in adaptation.

The theory also highlighted the relationship between structure, function and environment. Lamarck’s ideas encouraged later scientists to study adaptation and evolution more scientifically.

Demerits of Lamarckism

The main weakness of Lamarckism is that acquired characters are generally not inherited. Characters developed during the lifetime of an organism do not usually affect germ cells and therefore cannot be passed to offspring.

For example, cutting the tails of mice for many generations does not produce tailless mice. Similarly, muscles developed by exercise are not directly inherited by children.

Lamarck also did not know about genes, chromosomes, mutation and heredity. His idea of internal vital force is also not scientifically accepted. Therefore, Lamarckism is historically important but not accepted in its original form.

Theory 2

Darwinism

Meaning

Darwinism was proposed by Charles Darwin in his book On the Origin of Species in 1859. It is also called the theory of natural selection. Alfred Russel Wallace also independently proposed a similar idea.

Darwin was influenced by observations made during his voyage on HMS Beagle and by Malthus’ idea that populations increase faster than food supply. According to Darwin, organisms produce more offspring than can survive, and nature selects individuals with favourable variations.

1. Overproduction or Enormous Fertility

Darwin observed that all organisms have a very high reproductive capacity. They produce far more offspring than can survive. For example, plants produce thousands of seeds, fishes lay thousands of eggs and insects produce many offspring. If all offspring survived, the population of every species would increase enormously.

However, in nature, population size remains more or less limited because most offspring fail to survive due to competition, predation, disease or environmental stress. Thus, overproduction creates pressure on available resources.

2. Struggle for Existence

Since organisms produce more offspring than the environment can support, there is competition for food, space, light, water, mates and other resources. Darwin called this the struggle for existence.

In plants, seedlings may compete for light, minerals, water and space. Only some survive and grow into mature plants.

Type of struggleExplanation
Intraspecific struggleCompetition among individuals of the same species
Interspecific struggleCompetition between individuals of different species
Environmental struggleStruggle against physical factors like drought, flood, temperature, disease and climate

3. Variations

Darwin observed that individuals of the same species are not exactly alike. They show differences in size, shape, colour, physiology, resistance and reproductive capacity. These differences are called variations.

Some variations are favourable, some are harmful and some are neutral. Favourable variations help organisms survive better in a particular environment. For example, in a dry habitat, plants with deeper roots, smaller leaves or thicker cuticle may survive better. In a cold region, plants with suitable physiological tolerance may have an advantage.

Darwin considered variations as the raw material for evolution. However, he could not explain the actual origin of variations because genetics was not known at that time.

4. Survival of the Fittest

In the struggle for existence, individuals with favourable variations survive better than others. This is called survival of the fittest.

Fitness does not mean physical strength only. It means the ability of an organism to survive and reproduce successfully in a particular environment. For example, a plant with better drought tolerance may be “fitter” in a desert, while a fast-growing plant may be fitter in a disturbed habitat. Thus, fitness depends on the environment.

Individuals without useful adaptations may die early or reproduce less.

5. Natural Selection

Natural selection is the central idea of Darwinism. Nature acts as a selecting force. It preserves individuals with favourable variations and eliminates less adapted individuals.

Natural selection does not create new variations. It only acts on existing variations. Useful variations are passed to the next generation because individuals possessing them reproduce more successfully. Over many generations, favourable characters become more common in the population. This leads to adaptation.

For example, if drought-resistant plants survive better during repeated dry seasons, the next generations will contain more drought-resistant individuals.

6. Origin of New Species

Darwin believed that useful variations accumulate slowly over many generations. As the population changes, it becomes better adapted to its environment. If changes become large enough and populations become separated, new species may arise.

Sequence of Darwinism: Overproduction → struggle for existence → variations → survival of the fittest → natural selection → accumulation of favourable variations → origin of new species

Merits of Darwinism

Darwinism gave a natural and scientific explanation of evolution. It explained adaptation, struggle for existence, survival of the fittest and the origin of new species without depending on supernatural causes.

It is supported by many examples such as industrial melanism, pesticide resistance, antibiotic resistance and adaptation of plants to different habitats. The concept of natural selection remains one of the strongest foundations of evolutionary biology.

Demerits of Darwinism

Darwinism could not explain the origin and inheritance of variations because Mendel’s work was not known to Darwin. Darwin also gave more importance to continuous variations and did not properly explain sudden mutations.

He could not explain how favourable variations are genetically preserved and how reproductive isolation helps in speciation. These limitations were later corrected by Neo-Darwinism and Modern Synthetic Theory.

Theory 3

Neo-Darwinism

Meaning

Neo-Darwinism is a modified form of Darwinism. It accepts natural selection as an important force of evolution but rejects the inheritance of acquired characters. It was strongly supported by August Weismann.

Neo-Darwinism states that only heritable variations can take part in evolution.

1. Rejection of Acquired Characters

Neo-Darwinism rejects Lamarck’s idea that acquired characters are inherited. Changes produced in the body due to use, disuse or environment are not passed to the next generation unless they affect germ cells.

For example, if a person develops strong muscles by exercise, that character is not directly inherited by children.

Type of cellRole
Somatic cellsForm the body; changes are not inherited
Germ cellsForm gametes; changes can be inherited

2. Germplasm Theory

Weismann proposed the germplasm theory. According to it, the body has two types of cells: somatic cells and germ cells. Only changes in germ cells can be transmitted to offspring. Therefore, evolution depends on changes in the germplasm, not on changes acquired by the body.

3. Continuity of Germplasm

Weismann believed that germplasm continues from one generation to another through gametes. Somatic cells die with the body, but germ cells carry hereditary material to the next generation. This explained why acquired characters are not inherited.

4. Natural Selection Acts on Heritable Variations

Neo-Darwinism retains Darwin’s idea of natural selection but makes it clear that natural selection acts only on inherited variations. Non-heritable changes cannot cause evolution. Thus, Neo-Darwinism connects natural selection with heredity.

Importance and Limitations

Neo-Darwinism corrected Lamarckism and improved Darwinism by emphasizing germ cells and heritable variations. It prepared the way for modern evolutionary genetics.

However, it did not fully explain mutation, recombination, gene frequency, genetic drift, gene flow and population-level evolution. These were later explained by the Modern Synthetic Theory.

Theory 4

Modern Synthetic Theory

Meaning

The Modern Synthetic Theory is the most accepted theory of evolution. It combines Darwin’s natural selection with Mendelian genetics, mutation theory, population genetics, cytology and systematics.

Important contributors include Fisher, Haldane, Wright, Dobzhansky, Mayr, Simpson and Stebbins. According to this theory, evolution is a change in gene frequency in a population over generations.

1. Mutation

Mutation is a sudden heritable change in genetic material. It produces new alleles and is the ultimate source of new variation. Mutations may be harmful, neutral or beneficial. Beneficial mutations may be preserved by natural selection.

2. Recombination

Recombination produces new gene combinations during sexual reproduction. It occurs through crossing over, independent assortment of chromosomes and random fertilization. In plants, cross-pollination increases recombination by mixing genes from different individuals.

3. Natural Selection

Natural selection acts on genetic variations in a population. Individuals with favourable gene combinations survive and reproduce better. As a result, favourable alleles increase in frequency. Thus, natural selection changes the genetic composition of a population.

4. Genetic Drift

Genetic drift is random change in gene frequency, especially in small populations. It may cause some alleles to become common or disappear purely by chance. Founder effect and bottleneck effect are examples of genetic drift.

5. Gene Flow

Gene flow is the movement of genes between populations. In plants, it may occur through pollen transfer, seed dispersal or hybridization. Gene flow introduces new alleles and increases variation, but it may also reduce differences between populations.

6. Isolation

Isolation prevents interbreeding between populations. It may be geographical, ecological, seasonal, mechanical or genetic. When populations remain isolated, they accumulate different genetic changes. This is important for speciation.

7. Speciation

Speciation is the formation of new species. It occurs when populations become genetically different and reproductively isolated. In plants, speciation may occur through geographical isolation, hybridization and polyploidy. Polyploidy is especially important because chromosome doubling can quickly produce new species.

Summary of Modern Synthetic Theory

According to Modern Synthetic Theory: Mutation produces new alleles. Recombination creates new gene combinations. Natural selection preserves useful variations. Genetic drift and gene flow change gene frequencies. Isolation prevents interbreeding. Accumulation of genetic differences leads to speciation.

Thus, evolution is a population-level process involving changes in gene frequency.

The Modern Synthetic Theory gives the most complete explanation by combining natural selection with genetics, mutation, recombination, genetic drift, gene flow, isolation and speciation.

Variation and Phylogenetic Analysis

Evolution depends on two important ideas: variation and relationship among organisms. Variation provides the raw material for evolution, while phylogenetic analysis helps us understand evolutionary relationships and ancestry of organisms. Without variation, natural selection and evolution cannot occur. Without phylogenetic analysis, it is difficult to understand how different groups of organisms are related and how they evolved from common ancestors.

Variation

Variation

Definition

Variation refers to the differences found among individuals of the same species or among different species. No two individuals are exactly alike. These differences may occur in morphology, anatomy, physiology, cytology, biochemistry or genetics.

In evolution, variation is very important because it provides the material on which natural selection acts. Favourable variations are preserved, while harmful variations may be eliminated.

1. Somatic and Germinal Variation

Somatic variation

Somatic variation occurs in body cells. It may be caused by environmental factors such as light, temperature, nutrition, water availability or injury. These variations are generally not inherited because they do not affect the germ cells.

Example: A plant growing in shade may have larger, thinner leaves than the same species growing in strong sunlight.

Germinal variation

Germinal variation occurs in reproductive cells or germ cells. These variations are heritable and can be passed to the next generation. Germinal variations are important in evolution because they affect the genetic constitution of offspring.

Examples include mutations in gametes and genetic recombinations during meiosis.

2. Continuous and Discontinuous Variation

Continuous variation

Continuous variation shows small and gradual differences among individuals. It usually produces a range of forms without sharp breaks. It is commonly controlled by many genes and is also influenced by environment.

Examples: Height of plants, size of leaves, seed weight and flower size.

Discontinuous variation

Discontinuous variation shows distinct differences with no intermediate forms. It is usually controlled by one or few genes and is less influenced by environment.

Examples: Flower colour differences, presence or absence of awns in grasses and blood groups in animals.

3. Heritable and Environmental Variation

Heritable variation

Heritable variation is caused by changes in genes or chromosomes and is passed from parents to offspring. It is important for evolution and speciation.

Examples: Mutation, recombination, hybridization and polyploidy.

Environmental variation

Environmental variation is caused by external conditions such as soil, water, light, temperature and nutrition. These variations may affect the appearance of organisms but are usually not inherited.

Example: The same plant species may grow tall in fertile soil but remain stunted in poor soil.

Importance of Variation

Variation is the foundation of evolution. It allows some individuals to survive better under changing environmental conditions. Natural selection acts on variation and preserves favourable characters.

In plants, variation leads to the formation of ecotypes, varieties and species. It is also useful in plant breeding because breeders select useful variations such as high yield, disease resistance, drought tolerance and better fruit quality.

Thus, variation is important for adaptation, survival, natural selection, speciation and crop improvement.

Phylogeny

Phylogenetic Analysis

Definition

Phylogenetic analysis is the study of evolutionary relationships among organisms. It attempts to find out how different organisms are related to each other and how they evolved from common ancestors.

A phylogenetic analysis usually results in a branching diagram called a phylogenetic tree or cladogram. This diagram shows the possible evolutionary relationships among organisms.

Meaning of Phylogeny

Phylogeny means the evolutionary history of a group of organisms. It shows the line of descent and relationship among taxa.

For example, in plants, phylogenetic study helps us understand how algae, bryophytes, pteridophytes, gymnosperms and angiosperms are related and how advanced characters evolved through time.

Modern phylogenetic analysis gives great importance to molecular data because DNA and protein sequences provide direct information about genetic relationships.

Terms

Basic Terms Used in Phylogenetic Analysis

1. Taxon

A taxon is any unit used in classification, such as species, genus, family or order.

2. Character

A character is any feature used for comparison, such as leaf type, flower structure, chromosome number or DNA sequence.

3. Homology

Homologous characters have the same evolutionary origin but may perform different functions. Homology is very important in phylogenetic analysis because it indicates common ancestry.

Example: Leaf tendril of pea and leaf spine of cactus are homologous because both are modifications of leaves.

4. Analogy

Analogous characters have similar function but different origin. They may mislead phylogenetic analysis if not identified properly.

Example: Leaf tendril of pea and stem tendril of Cucurbita both help in climbing but have different origins.

5. Clade

A clade is a group of organisms consisting of a common ancestor and all its descendants.

6. Outgroup

An outgroup is a related taxon used for comparison with the main group under study. It helps in identifying primitive and advanced characters.

Methods

Methods of Phylogenetic Analysis

1. Phenetic Method

The phenetic method groups organisms based on overall similarity. It considers many characters and measures the total resemblance among organisms. However, it may not always show true evolutionary relationships because similarity may be due to analogy or convergence.

2. Cladistic Method

The cladistic method groups organisms based on shared derived characters. It tries to identify common ancestry and branching patterns. It produces a diagram called a cladogram.

3. Molecular Phylogenetic Method

Molecular phylogeny uses DNA, RNA or protein sequences to study relationships. Organisms with more similar sequences are considered more closely related. This method is widely used in modern taxonomy and evolutionary biology.

Relationship Between Variation and Phylogenetic AnalysisVariation and phylogenetic analysis are closely related. Variation produces differences among organisms. Some variations become inherited and accumulate over generations. These accumulated variations create differences between populations, species and higher groups. Phylogenetic analysis studies these differences and similarities to reconstruct evolutionary relationships. Homologous variations help in identifying common ancestry, while derived variations help in understanding evolutionary branching. Thus, variation provides the material for evolution, and phylogenetic analysis helps interpret the evolutionary history produced by that variation.
ConclusionVariation refers to differences among organisms and is the raw material of evolution. It may be somatic or germinal, continuous or discontinuous, heritable or environmental. Heritable variation arises through mutation, recombination, hybridization and polyploidy and plays an important role in natural selection and speciation. Phylogenetic analysis is the study of evolutionary relationships among organisms. It uses morphological, anatomical, embryological, cytological, palynological, biochemical, molecular and fossil evidence to construct phylogenetic trees. Thus, variation explains how organisms change, while phylogenetic analysis explains how organisms are related through evolution.
