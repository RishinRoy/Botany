Structure of Anther and Pollen

In angiosperms, the stamen is the male reproductive organ of the flower. It consists of a filament and an anther. The anther produces pollen grains, and each pollen grain represents the male gametophyte. The study of the structure of anther and pollen is important because it explains the formation, protection and transfer of male gametes during sexual reproduction.

Structure of Anther

A typical angiosperm anther is usually bilobed and dithecous. This means it has two lobes, and each lobe has two pollen sacs or microsporangia. Therefore, a mature anther usually contains four microsporangia.

The two anther lobes are connected by a sterile tissue called the connective. The connective also contains the vascular bundle which supplies food and water to the anther.

T.S. of Young Anther

In transverse section, a young anther shows four pollen sacs. Each pollen sac is surrounded by an anther wall. Inside each pollen sac, pollen mother cells are present. These pollen mother cells later undergo meiosis to form microspores.

The wall of a young anther consists of four layers: epidermis, endothecium, middle layers and tapetum.

Wall Layers of Anther

1. Epidermis

The epidermis is the outermost layer of the anther wall. It is single-layered and protective in function. It protects the inner tissues of the anther during early development. As the anther matures, the epidermis may become stretched and flattened.

2. Endothecium

The endothecium lies just below the epidermis. It is a very important layer because it helps in the dehiscence of anther.

The cells of endothecium develop fibrous thickenings on their inner and radial walls. These thickenings are hygroscopic. When the anther dries at maturity, the endothecium helps in splitting the anther wall so that pollen grains can be released.

3. Middle Layers

The middle layers are present between the endothecium and tapetum. They are usually one to three layers thick. Their cells are temporary and usually degenerate during anther maturation.

The middle layers help in nourishment during the early stages of pollen development, but they disappear by the time the anther becomes mature.

4. Tapetum

The tapetum is the innermost layer of the anther wall and surrounds the sporogenous tissue. It is the most important nutritive layer of the anther.

The tapetal cells are rich in cytoplasm and may be multinucleate. They provide nutrition to the developing microspores and pollen grains. The tapetum also helps in the formation of pollen wall materials, especially sporopollenin, which contributes to the exine of pollen grains.

Type of tapetumCharacter
Secretory or glandular tapetumTapetal cells remain in position and secrete nutrients into the pollen sac.
Amoeboid or invasive tapetumTapetal cells break down and form a periplasmodium around developing microspores.

Sporogenous Tissue and Microspore Formation

Inside each pollen sac, there is a mass of sporogenous tissue. The cells of this tissue become microspore mother cells or pollen mother cells. Each microspore mother cell undergoes meiosis and forms four haploid microspores. These four microspores are usually arranged in a tetrad. Later, the microspores separate from each other and develop into pollen grains. Thus, pollen grains are formed inside the microsporangia of the anther.

Dehiscence of Anther

At maturity, the anther dries and opens to release pollen grains. This opening of the anther is called dehiscence. The endothecium plays the main role in dehiscence due to its fibrous thickenings. The anther commonly dehisces through a longitudinal slit. After dehiscence, pollen grains are released and become available for pollination.

Structure of Pollen Grain

A pollen grain is the male gametophyte of angiosperms. It develops from a microspore. A mature pollen grain is usually small, spherical or oval, and surrounded by a two-layered wall.

The pollen wall consists of two layers: exine and intine.

1. Exine

The exine is the outer thick wall of the pollen grain. It is made of a highly resistant substance called sporopollenin. Sporopollenin is very resistant to heat, chemicals and biological decomposition.

The exine may show different types of sculpturing such as spines, ridges, reticulations or pores. These surface patterns are important in pollen identification and taxonomy. The exine is not continuous everywhere. It has thin areas called germ pores or germ apertures. The pollen tube comes out through one of these germ pores during germination.

2. Intine

The intine is the inner thin wall of the pollen grain. It is made mainly of cellulose and pectin. It is delicate and lies inner to the exine. During pollen germination, the intine grows out through the germ pore to form the pollen tube. The pollen tube carries the male gametes towards the embryo sac.

Cells of Mature Pollen Grain

At the time of release, pollen grain may be two-celled or three-celled.

In most angiosperms, pollen is released at the two-celled stage. It contains a large vegetative cell and a small generative cell. The vegetative cell or tube cell controls pollen tube formation. The generative cell later divides mitotically to form two male gametes.

In some plants, the generative cell divides before pollen shedding. In such cases, the pollen grain is released at the three-celled stage, containing one vegetative cell and two male gametes.

Germination of Pollen

After pollination, the pollen grain lands on a suitable stigma. It absorbs moisture and germinates. The intine grows out through the germ pore to form a pollen tube. The vegetative nucleus enters the pollen tube, followed by the generative cell or male gametes.

The pollen tube grows through the style and reaches the ovule. It carries the two male gametes to the embryo sac for fertilization.

A pollen grain has two walls, exine and intine, and usually contains a vegetative cell and a generative cell. The pollen grain germinates on the stigma and forms a pollen tube, which carries male gametes for fertilization. Therefore, both anther and pollen are essential structures in the sexual reproduction of angiosperms.

Microsporogenesis and Development of Male Gametophyte

In angiosperms, the male reproductive organ is the stamen, which consists of filament and anther. The anther contains microsporangia or pollen sacs. Inside the microsporangia, microspores are formed by meiosis from microspore mother cells. These microspores later develop into pollen grains, which represent the male gametophyte. The formation of microspores is called microsporogenesis, while the development of the male gametophyte from the microspore is called microgametogenesis.

Main heading

1. Microsporogenesis

Microsporogenesis is the process by which haploid microspores are formed from diploid microspore mother cells through meiosis inside the microsporangium of the anther. The microspores later develop into pollen grains.

Development of Microsporangium

A young anther is usually bilobed and dithecous, having four microsporangia or pollen sacs. Each microsporangium contains sporogenous tissue. During development, some hypodermal cells of the young anther become distinct and function as archesporial cells. Each archesporial cell divides periclinally to form two cells: a primary parietal cell and a primary sporogenous cell.

Cell formedFunction
Primary parietal cellForms the anther wall layers.
Primary sporogenous cellForms sporogenous tissue.

The primary parietal cells divide and form the wall layers of the anther: epidermis, endothecium, middle layers and tapetum. The primary sporogenous cells divide to form sporogenous tissue.

Microspore Mother Cells

The cells of the sporogenous tissue become rounded and separate from each other. They function as microspore mother cells or pollen mother cells. Each microspore mother cell is diploid and lies inside the pollen sac. Each pollen mother cell is surrounded by a callose wall, which separates it from neighbouring cells.

Meiosis and Formation of Microspore Tetrad

Each microspore mother cell undergoes two meiotic divisions. During the first meiotic division, two haploid nuclei are formed. During the second meiotic division, four haploid nuclei are produced. Cytokinesis then results in the formation of four haploid microspores arranged in a group called a microspore tetrad.

The common types of microspore tetrads are tetrahedral, isobilateral, decussate and linear. The tetrahedral type is very common in dicot plants.

Later, the callose wall surrounding the tetrad dissolves due to the enzyme callase secreted by the tapetum. As a result, the four microspores separate from one another. Each separated microspore develops into a pollen grain.

2. Structure of Young Microspore

A young microspore is haploid and usually spherical. It has dense cytoplasm and a prominent nucleus. Later, a two-layered wall develops around it. The outer wall is called exine, and the inner wall is called intine.

The exine is thick and made of sporopollenin. It is highly resistant to heat, chemicals and decay. The intine is thin and made mainly of cellulose and pectin. The exine has thin areas called germ pores or germ apertures, through which the pollen tube emerges during germination.

Main heading

3. Development of Male Gametophyte

The development of the male gametophyte from a microspore or pollen grain is called microgametogenesis. In angiosperms, the male gametophyte is highly reduced and is represented by the pollen grain and the pollen tube containing male gametes.

First Mitotic Division of Microspore

The microspore increases in size and its nucleus divides mitotically. This division is unequal and produces two unequal cells: a large vegetative cell or tube cell and a small generative cell.

The vegetative cell has abundant cytoplasm and a large nucleus. It controls the formation and growth of the pollen tube. The generative cell is small, spindle-shaped or lens-shaped and lies within the cytoplasm of the vegetative cell. It later gives rise to the two male gametes. At this stage, the pollen grain is called a two-celled pollen grain.

Two-celled and Three-celled Pollen

In most angiosperms, pollen grains are shed at the two-celled stage. This means the mature pollen grain at the time of release contains one vegetative cell and one generative cell.

In some plants, the generative cell divides before pollen shedding and forms two male gametes. In such cases, pollen is shed at the three-celled stage.

Type of pollen at sheddingCells present
Two-celled pollenOne vegetative cell and one generative cell
Three-celled pollenOne vegetative cell and two male gametes

Division of Generative Cell

If the pollen grain is shed at the two-celled stage, the generative cell divides later, usually inside the pollen tube. It divides mitotically to form two male gametes or sperm cells. These two male gametes are non-motile and are carried by the pollen tube to the embryo sac.

Pollen Germination and Pollen Tube Formation

After pollination, the pollen grain lands on a suitable stigma. It absorbs water and nutrients from the stigmatic surface and germinates. The intine grows out through a germ pore to form the pollen tube. The vegetative nucleus enters the pollen tube and controls its growth. The generative cell also enters the pollen tube and divides into two male gametes if it has not divided earlier.

The pollen tube grows through the style and enters the ovule, usually through the micropyle. It finally reaches the embryo sac and releases the two male gametes.

4. Male Gametophyte of Angiosperms

The mature male gametophyte is very small and highly reduced. It consists of one vegetative cell or tube cell and two male gametes. The vegetative cell forms the pollen tube, while the two male gametes take part in double fertilization. One male gamete fuses with the egg to form the zygote, and the other fuses with the secondary nucleus to form the primary endosperm nucleus.

SignificanceMicrosporogenesis is important because it produces haploid microspores through meiosis and maintains chromosome number during sexual reproduction. The development of the male gametophyte is important because it produces the male gametes and ensures their transfer to the embryo sac through the pollen tube. The reduction of the male gametophyte and formation of pollen tube are advanced features of angiosperms.
ConclusionMicrosporogenesis is the formation of haploid microspores from diploid microspore mother cells by meiosis inside the anther. Each microspore mother cell gives rise to a tetrad of four microspores, which later separate and develop into pollen grains. The microspore undergoes mitotic division to form a large vegetative cell and a small generative cell. The generative cell divides to form two male gametes, while the vegetative cell forms the pollen tube. Thus, the male gametophyte of angiosperms is highly reduced and is represented by the pollen grain and pollen tube containing two male gametes.

Pollen Morphology and NPC System

The branch of botany dealing with the study of pollen grains and spores is called palynology. Pollen grains are the male gametophytes of seed plants and are produced inside the anther. They are generally microscopic, but they show great variation in structure, size, shape, polarity, symmetry, aperture and wall ornamentation. These characters together are called pollen morphology.

Pollen morphology is important because pollen characters are usually stable and are useful in plant identification, taxonomy, phylogeny, fossil studies, honey analysis and allergy studies.

Pollen Wall or Sporoderm

The wall of a pollen grain is called the sporoderm. It consists of two main layers: exine and intine.

Exine

The exine is the outer thick and resistant wall of the pollen grain. It is made up of sporopollenin, which is highly resistant to chemical and biological decomposition. Because of this, pollen grains are often preserved as fossils.

The exine is divided into two main layers: sexine, the outer sculptured layer, and nexine, the inner non-sculptured layer. The sexine may show structures such as tectum, columellae and foot layer. The surface of the exine may be smooth or ornamented. These ornamentations are important for pollen identification.

Intine

The intine is the inner, thin and delicate wall of the pollen grain. It is mainly made up of cellulose and pectin, so it is pectocellulosic in nature. During pollen germination, the intine comes out through the aperture and forms the pollen tube.

The intine may be differentiated into ectintine and endintine.

Morphological Characteristics of Pollen Grains

The main morphological characters of pollen grains are pollen units, polarity, symmetry, shape, size and aperture.

1. Pollen Units

Pollen grains may be shed singly or in groups. On this basis, different pollen units are recognized.

Pollen unitDescription
MonadSingle pollen grain
DyadTwo pollen grains together
TetradFour pollen grains together
PolyadMany pollen grains together
PolliniumCompact mass of pollen grains

Most pollen grains are released as monads. In some plants, pollen grains remain united in groups. Tetrads may be of different types such as tetrahedral tetrad, tetragonal tetrad, rhomboidal tetrad, T-shaped tetrad, linear tetrad, transverse tetrad and cryptotetrad. In orchids and some members of Asclepiadaceae, pollen grains are grouped into compact masses called pollinia.

2. Polarity

Polarity is an important character in identification and description of pollen grains. Pollen grains are formed in the tetrad stage during development. The side of the pollen grain facing the centre of the tetrad is called the proximal pole. The side facing away from the centre of the tetrad is called the distal pole.

The imaginary line joining the proximal and distal poles is called the polar axis. The plane at right angles to the polar axis and passing through the middle of the pollen grain is called the equatorial plane. The diameter along this plane is called the equatorial diameter.

TypeCharacter
ApolarPoles cannot be distinguished
PolarPoles are clearly distinguishable
IsopolarProximal and distal faces are similar
HeteropolarProximal and distal faces are different

In isopolar pollen, both faces look alike. In heteropolar pollen, the two faces differ in shape, aperture system or ornamentation. Some pollen grains show only slight difference between the two faces and may be called para-isopolar or sub-isopolar.

3. Symmetry

Pollen grains may be symmetrical or asymmetrical. Symmetry depends on whether a pollen grain can be divided into equal halves by one or more planes.

TypeDescription
AsymmetricalNo definite plane of symmetry
RadiosymmetricCan be divided into equal halves by several planes passing through the polar axis
BilateralCan be divided into equal halves by only one plane

Radiosymmetric pollen grains are radially symmetrical, while bilateral pollen grains have only one plane of symmetry.

4. Shape of Pollen Grain

The shape of pollen grain is studied mainly in two views: polar view and equatorial view. Shape is also described by the ratio of polar axis (P) and equatorial diameter (E).

A. Shape in Polar View

When the pollen grain is observed from the polar side, it is called polar view. In polar view, pollen grains may be non-angular, such as circular or elliptic, or angular, such as triangular, quadrangular or quinquangular. In angular pollen grains, the angles may be acute or obtuse. The sides may be concave, straight or convex.

B. Shape in Equatorial View

When the pollen grain is observed from the side, it is called equatorial view. In equatorial view, pollen grains may be non-angular, such as circular or elliptic, or angular, such as rectangular or rhombic. The ends of elliptic or elongated pollen may be acute, acuminate, emerginate, truncate or obtuse.

Pollen grains vary greatly in size. Size is measured in micrometres. According to Erdtman, pollen grains are divided into different size classes.

6. Apertures

Apertures are thin or weak regions in the pollen wall. They are also called germination zones, because the pollen tube usually emerges through them during pollen germination. Some pollen grains have no aperture and are called inaperturate or atreme. In aperturate pollen, apertures may differ in number, position and character.

Importance of Pollen Morphology and NPC SystemPollen morphology and the NPC system are important in plant taxonomy because pollen characters are usually constant and diagnostic. Characters such as pollen unit, polarity, symmetry, shape, size, aperture type and exine ornamentation help in identifying plants. They are also useful in evolutionary studies. For example, monosulcate pollen is common in many monocots and primitive angiosperms, whereas tricolpate pollen is an important character of eudicots. Pollen grains are also important in palaeobotany because their resistant exine helps them remain preserved as fossils. Pollen studies are useful in aeropalynology, allergy studies, forensic science and melissopalynology, which is the study of pollen in honey.
ConclusionPollen morphology includes the study of pollen wall, pollen units, polarity, symmetry, shape, size and apertures. The pollen wall consists of exine and intine. Exine is resistant and often ornamented, while intine forms the pollen tube during germination. Pollen grains may occur as monads, tetrads, polyads or pollinia. They also vary in polarity, symmetry, shape and size. Apertures act as germination zones and are described by the NPC system of Erdtman, which is based on number, position and character of apertures. Therefore, pollen morphology and NPC system are important tools in taxonomy, identification and evolutionary studies.

Structure and Types of Ovules

The ovule is the megasporangium of seed plants. In angiosperms, ovules are present inside the ovary and are attached to the placenta. After fertilization, the ovule develops into a seed. Therefore, the ovule is an important reproductive structure because it contains the female gametophyte or embryo sac.

A typical ovule consists of a stalk, protective coverings, nucellus and embryo sac. Ovules show different types depending on the position of the micropyle, chalaza and funicle.

Structure of a Typical Ovule

A mature angiosperm ovule consists of the following parts: funicle, hilum, raphe, integuments, micropyle, nucellus, embryo sac and chalaza.

1. Funicle

The funicle is the stalk of the ovule. It attaches the ovule to the placenta inside the ovary. It also conducts food and water from the placenta to the developing ovule.

2. Hilum

The point where the funicle is attached to the body of the ovule is called the hilum. It represents the junction between the ovule and its stalk. After seed formation, the hilum remains as a scar on the seed coat.

3. Raphe

In some ovules, especially in anatropous ovules, the funicle becomes fused with the body of the ovule along one side. This fused ridge is called the raphe. It helps in the conduction of nutrients from the funicle towards the chalazal region.

4. Integuments

The ovule is surrounded by one or two protective coverings called integuments. These integuments later form the seed coat after fertilization.

TypeCharacter
UnitegmicOvule with one integument
BitegmicOvule with two integuments
AtegmicOvule without integument

Most angiosperm ovules are bitegmic, having an outer and an inner integument.

5. Micropyle

The integuments do not completely cover the nucellus. They leave a small opening at one end called the micropyle. The pollen tube usually enters the ovule through the micropyle during fertilization. After the ovule becomes a seed, the micropyle may remain as a small pore through which water enters during seed germination.

6. Nucellus

The nucellus is the main body of the ovule. It is a mass of parenchymatous tissue enclosed by the integuments. It represents the megasporangium of the ovule. The nucellus nourishes the developing embryo sac.

In some plants, the nucellus is thin and mostly consumed during embryo sac development. In others, it remains well developed. Based on nucellus development, ovules may be tenuinucellate or crassinucellate.

7. Embryo Sac

The embryo sac is the female gametophyte of angiosperms. It develops inside the nucellus from the functional megaspore. A typical mature embryo sac is 7-celled and 8-nucleate. It has one egg cell and two synergids at the micropylar end, two polar nuclei or secondary nucleus in the central cell, and three antipodal cells at the chalazal end.

8. Chalaza

The chalaza is the basal region of the ovule, opposite the micropyle. It is the region where the nucellus and integuments merge. Vascular supply usually reaches up to the chalazal region and then nutrients pass into the nucellus and embryo sac.

Types of Ovules

Ovules are classified mainly on the basis of the position of the micropyle, chalaza and funicle. The important types of ovules are orthotropous, anatropous, campylotropous, amphitropous and circinotropous.

1. Orthotropous Ovule

The orthotropous ovule is the simplest and straight type of ovule. In this type, the micropyle, chalaza and funicle lie in one straight line. The body of the ovule is not curved. The micropyle is present at the upper end and the chalaza lies at the basal end. This type is considered primitive.

Example: Polygonum, Piperaceae.

2. Anatropous Ovule

The anatropous ovule is the most common type of ovule in angiosperms. In this type, the body of the ovule becomes completely inverted during development. As a result, the micropyle comes close to the funicle. The funicle becomes fused with the body of the ovule along one side, forming the raphe.

The chalaza lies at the opposite end. This type is common in most flowering plants.

Example: Pea, sunflower, most angiosperms.

3. Campylotropous Ovule

In a campylotropous ovule, the body of the ovule is curved. Due to this curvature, the micropyle comes close to the chalaza. However, the embryo sac usually remains almost straight. This type is commonly seen in some members of Cruciferae.

Example: Capsella, mustard.

4. Amphitropous Ovule

In an amphitropous ovule, both the ovule body and the embryo sac are curved. The curvature is more pronounced than in campylotropous ovule. Due to this curvature, the embryo sac may appear horse-shoe shaped.

Example: Alisma.

5. Circinotropous Ovule

In a circinotropous ovule, the funicle grows around the ovule in a circular manner. The ovule first bends, and later, due to continued growth of the funicle, it becomes almost encircled by the funicle. This is a special type of ovule.

Example: Opuntia.

Significance of Ovule

The ovule is very important in sexual reproduction of angiosperms. It contains the embryo sac, which bears the egg cell, synergids, polar nuclei and antipodal cells. Fertilization takes place inside the ovule.

After fertilization, the ovule develops into a seed. The integuments develop into the seed coat, the zygote develops into the embryo and the primary endosperm nucleus develops into the endosperm. Thus, the ovule is directly involved in seed formation and continuation of the plant life cycle.

ConclusionThe ovule is the megasporangium of angiosperms and is borne inside the ovary on the placenta. A typical ovule consists of funicle, hilum, raphe, integuments, micropyle, nucellus, embryo sac and chalaza. Based on the position of micropyle, chalaza and funicle, ovules are mainly classified into orthotropous, anatropous, campylotropous, amphitropous and circinotropous types. Among these, the anatropous ovule is the most common type in angiosperms. After fertilization, the ovule develops into a seed, making it one of the most important structures in plant reproduction.

Megasporogenesis and Development of Female Gametophyte

In angiosperms, the ovule is the structure inside which the female gametophyte develops. The female gametophyte is also called the embryo sac. It develops inside the nucellus of the ovule from a functional megaspore. The formation of haploid megaspores from the diploid megaspore mother cell is called megasporogenesis. The development of the female gametophyte or embryo sac from the functional megaspore is called megagametogenesis.

In most angiosperms, the embryo sac develops from a single functional megaspore. This type is called the monosporic type, and the commonest example is the Polygonum type of embryo sac.

Main heading

1. Megasporogenesis

Megasporogenesis is the process by which haploid megaspores are formed from a diploid megaspore mother cell by meiosis inside the nucellus of the ovule. It occurs in the ovule, usually near the micropylar region of the nucellus.

Development of Megaspore Mother Cell

In the young ovule, a single hypodermal cell of the nucellus becomes distinct. This cell is called the archesporial cell. The archesporial cell is larger than the surrounding cells and has dense cytoplasm and a prominent nucleus.

In many angiosperms, the archesporial cell directly functions as the megaspore mother cell. In some plants, it first divides into a primary parietal cell and a primary sporogenous cell. The primary sporogenous cell functions as the megaspore mother cell. The megaspore mother cell is diploid and lies inside the nucellus.

Meiosis in Megaspore Mother Cell

The megaspore mother cell undergoes meiosis. As a result, four haploid megaspores are formed. These four megaspores are usually arranged in a linear tetrad. The tetrad has one megaspore towards the micropylar end, two middle megaspores and one megaspore towards the chalazal end.

In most angiosperms, only one megaspore remains functional, usually the chalazal megaspore. The other three megaspores degenerate. Thus, out of four megaspores, only one takes part in the formation of the female gametophyte.

2. Development of Female Gametophyte

The development of the female gametophyte or embryo sac from the functional megaspore is called megagametogenesis. The functional megaspore develops into the embryo sac by mitotic divisions.

Polygonum Type of Embryo Sac

The Polygonum type is the most common type of embryo sac development in angiosperms. It is called monosporic because only one megaspore, usually the chalazal megaspore, develops into the embryo sac. A mature Polygonum type embryo sac is 7-celled, 8-nucleate and monosporic.

First Mitotic Division

The functional megaspore increases in size. Its nucleus divides mitotically to form two nuclei. One nucleus moves towards the micropylar end and the other towards the chalazal end. A large vacuole appears between them. This results in a two-nucleate embryo sac.

Second Mitotic Division

Both nuclei divide mitotically again. As a result, four nuclei are formed. Two nuclei are present at the micropylar end and two nuclei at the chalazal end. This is called the four-nucleate embryo sac stage.

Third Mitotic Division

The four nuclei divide once more by mitosis. This produces eight nuclei. Four nuclei are present at the micropylar end and four nuclei at the chalazal end. This is called the eight-nucleate embryo sac stage.

Organization of Mature Embryo Sac

After the formation of eight nuclei, the embryo sac becomes organized into cells. The mature embryo sac contains seven cells and eight nuclei.

1. Egg Apparatus

At the micropylar end, three cells are organized to form the egg apparatus. It consists of one egg cell and two synergids. The egg cell is the female gamete. It fuses with one male gamete during fertilization to form the zygote. The two synergids are present on either side of the egg cell. They help in guiding the pollen tube into the embryo sac. The synergids possess special finger-like wall thickenings called the filiform apparatus. The filiform apparatus helps in absorption and in directing the pollen tube.

2. Antipodal Cells

At the chalazal end, three cells are formed. These are called antipodal cells. The antipodal cells are usually small and short-lived. They may help in nutrition of the embryo sac. In some plants, they may become enlarged or persistent.

3. Central Cell

One nucleus from the micropylar end and one nucleus from the chalazal end move towards the centre of the embryo sac. These are called polar nuclei. The two polar nuclei may fuse to form a secondary nucleus. The central cell is therefore large and contains two polar nuclei or one secondary nucleus. During double fertilization, the second male gamete fuses with the polar nuclei or secondary nucleus to form the primary endosperm nucleus.

Mature Female Gametophyte

The mature female gametophyte of most angiosperms is therefore a 7-celled and 8-nucleate embryo sac. It contains three cells at the micropylar end, three cells at the chalazal end, and one large central cell.

Types of Embryo Sac, Organisation and Ultrastructure of Mature Embryo Sac

In angiosperms, the female gametophyte is called the embryo sac. It develops inside the nucellus of the ovule from the functional megaspore. The embryo sac contains the female gamete or egg cell and other associated cells which help in fertilization and seed formation.

The most common embryo sac in angiosperms is the Polygonum type, which is monosporic, 7-celled and 8-nucleate. However, embryo sacs may be of different types depending on the number of megaspore nuclei taking part in their development.

Types of Embryo Sac

Embryo sacs are classified mainly on the basis of the number of megaspores or megaspore nuclei involved in their development. They are of three main types: monosporic embryo sac, bisporic embryo sac and tetrasporic embryo sac.

1. Monosporic Embryo Sac

In the monosporic type, the embryo sac develops from only one functional megaspore. During megasporogenesis, the megaspore mother cell undergoes meiosis and forms four megaspores. Out of these four, three degenerate and only one remains functional. The functional megaspore undergoes mitotic divisions and forms the embryo sac.

The most common example is the Polygonum type embryo sac. It develops from the chalazal megaspore. The functional megaspore undergoes three mitotic divisions to form eight nuclei. Later, these nuclei organize into a 7-celled and 8-nucleate embryo sac. It contains one egg cell, two synergids, three antipodal cells and one central cell with two polar nuclei.

2. Bisporic Embryo Sac

In the bisporic type, two megaspore nuclei take part in embryo sac formation. Here, meiosis of the megaspore mother cell is not followed by complete wall formation between all four nuclei. As a result, two nuclei remain in one cell and develop further to form the embryo sac. Thus, the embryo sac develops from a binucleate megaspore cell.

Examples include Allium type and Endymion type. Since two meiotic nuclei contribute to the formation of the embryo sac, this type is called bisporic.

3. Tetrasporic Embryo Sac

In the tetrasporic type, all four megaspore nuclei formed after meiosis take part in the formation of the embryo sac. Here, wall formation does not occur after meiosis, so all four haploid nuclei remain in a common cytoplasm. These nuclei then undergo further mitotic divisions and organize into the embryo sac.

Examples include Fritillaria type, Peperomia type, Drusa type, Adoxa type and Plumbago type. Since all four megaspore nuclei participate in embryo sac formation, this type is called tetrasporic.

Organisation of Mature Embryo Sac

The mature embryo sac of most angiosperms is of the Polygonum type. It is usually 7-celled and 8-nucleate. It is organized into three main regions: micropylar end, central region and chalazal end.

1. Micropylar End: Egg Apparatus

At the micropylar end, three cells are present. These three cells together form the egg apparatus. The egg apparatus consists of one egg cell and two synergids. The egg cell is the female gamete. It fuses with one male gamete during fertilization to form the zygote. The synergids are present on either side of the egg cell. They help in attracting and guiding the pollen tube into the embryo sac.

2. Central Region: Central Cell

The central region of the embryo sac contains a large central cell. It has two polar nuclei, one from the micropylar side and one from the chalazal side. These two polar nuclei may fuse to form a secondary nucleus. During double fertilization, the second male gamete fuses with the polar nuclei or secondary nucleus to form the primary endosperm nucleus.

3. Chalazal End: Antipodal Cells

At the chalazal end, three cells are present. These are called antipodal cells. The antipodal cells are usually small and short-lived. They may help in nutrition of the embryo sac. In some plants, they become enlarged and persistent.

Ultrastructure of Mature Embryo Sac

The mature embryo sac is not just a simple group of cells. Each cell is structurally specialized according to its function. The main cells are egg cell, synergids, central cell and antipodal cells.

1. Ultrastructure of Egg Cell

The egg cell is situated at the micropylar end between the two synergids. It is the female gamete of angiosperms. The egg cell is usually pear-shaped or oval. It has a thin wall, dense cytoplasm and a prominent nucleus. The nucleus is generally located towards the chalazal side, while a large vacuole is usually present towards the micropylar side.

The egg cell contains mitochondria, plastids, ribosomes and endoplasmic reticulum. These organelles help in maintaining the metabolic activity of the egg cell. The egg cell fuses with one male gamete to form the zygote, which later develops into the embryo.

2. Ultrastructure of Synergids

The two synergids lie on either side of the egg cell at the micropylar end. They are highly specialized cells. The most characteristic feature of synergids is the presence of the filiform apparatus at their micropylar end. The filiform apparatus consists of finger-like wall ingrowths. It increases the surface area of the synergid cells.

Synergids have dense cytoplasm, prominent nucleus, mitochondria, plastids, endoplasmic reticulum and dictyosomes. These features indicate that synergids are metabolically active. One synergid usually degenerates before or during the entry of the pollen tube. The pollen tube commonly enters the embryo sac through this degenerating synergid.

Functions: attracting the pollen tube, guiding the pollen tube into the embryo sac, absorption and transfer of nutrients, and receiving the pollen tube during fertilization.

3. Ultrastructure of Central Cell

The central cell is the largest cell of the embryo sac. It occupies the major central region. It contains two polar nuclei or a single secondary nucleus formed by fusion of the polar nuclei. The central cell has a large vacuole and cytoplasm concentrated around the nucleus and along the cell wall.

It contains mitochondria, plastids, ribosomes and endoplasmic reticulum. The central cell is metabolically active because it takes part in endosperm formation. During double fertilization, one male gamete fuses with the polar nuclei or secondary nucleus to form the primary endosperm nucleus. This later develops into the endosperm, which nourishes the embryo.

4. Ultrastructure of Antipodal Cells

The three antipodal cells are present at the chalazal end of the embryo sac. They usually have dense cytoplasm and prominent nuclei. In many plants, antipodal cells are short-lived and degenerate soon after fertilization. In some plants, especially grasses, they become enlarged, persistent and may even multiply.

Antipodal cells may contain mitochondria, plastids, ribosomes and other cytoplasmic organelles, showing their nutritive role. The main function of antipodal cells is believed to be nourishment of the embryo sac, especially from the chalazal region.

Significance of Mature Embryo SacThe mature embryo sac is essential for sexual reproduction in angiosperms. It contains the egg cell, which forms the zygote after fertilization. It also contains the central cell, which forms endosperm after triple fusion. Synergids guide the pollen tube and help in successful fertilization. Antipodal cells may help in nutrition. Thus, the embryo sac is directly involved in fertilization, embryo formation and endosperm formation.
ConclusionThe embryo sac is the female gametophyte of angiosperms. Based on the number of megaspores or megaspore nuclei involved in development, embryo sacs are of three main types: monosporic, bisporic and tetrasporic. The most common type is the monosporic Polygonum type, which is 7-celled and 8-nucleate. A mature embryo sac is organized into an egg apparatus at the micropylar end, a central cell in the middle and antipodal cells at the chalazal end. Ultrastructurally, the egg cell is specialized for fertilization, synergids show filiform apparatus for pollen tube guidance, the central cell is involved in endosperm formation and antipodal cells help in nutrition. Therefore, the mature embryo sac is a highly organized and functionally specialized structure essential for reproduction in angiosperms.
