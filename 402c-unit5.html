Anatomy of Flowering Plants and Its Significance

Anatomy is the branch of science dealing with the internal organization and structure of living things. In botany, plant anatomy studies the internal organization of flowering plants, especially the arrangement of tissues and tissue systems inside roots, stems and leaves.

Meaning of Plant Anatomy

Plant anatomy deals with the study of internal structure of plants. It explains how cells are organized into tissues and how tissues are arranged to form organs such as roots, stems, leaves and reproductive parts.

A tissue is a group of cells having a common origin and usually performing a common function. The study of tissues, tissue systems, secondary growth and anatomical adaptations is important for understanding the internal organization of flowering plants.

Why Plant Anatomy Is Studied

  1. Plant systematics and identification: anatomical characters help in identifying plants and supporting classification.
  2. Detection of adulterated products: internal characters help identify genuine and adulterated plant materials.
  3. Pharmacognosy: anatomy helps in studying crude drugs of natural origin and their quality.
  4. Dendrochronology and dendroclimatology: annual growth rings help in dating wood and studying past climatic conditions.
  5. Forensic science: plant tissues, fibres, pollen and wood fragments may help in investigation and identification.
  6. Wood anatomy: anatomical characters help distinguish superior and inferior woods.

Leaf Anatomy

A leaf is a lateral, flattened green organ mainly concerned with photosynthesis, transpiration and gaseous exchange. Internally, a leaf is made up of three main tissue systems: epidermis, mesophyll and vascular bundles. Leaf anatomy varies in dicots, monocots and xerophytes according to function and habitat.

1. Epidermis

The leaf has an upper epidermis and a lower epidermis. The epidermis is usually single-layered and covered by a cuticle, which reduces water loss. Stomata are present in the epidermis and help in gaseous exchange and transpiration.

In dicot leaves, stomata are generally more numerous on the lower epidermis, while in monocot leaves they may occur on both surfaces.

2. Mesophyll

The ground tissue between the two epidermal layers is called mesophyll. It contains chloroplasts and performs photosynthesis.

In a typical dicot leaf, the mesophyll is differentiated into palisade parenchyma and spongy parenchyma. Palisade tissue lies below the upper epidermis, has elongated compact cells and contains many chloroplasts. Spongy tissue lies towards the lower epidermis, has loosely arranged cells and large intercellular spaces, helping in gaseous exchange.

In monocot leaves, the mesophyll is usually not differentiated into palisade and spongy tissue.

3. Vascular Bundles

The veins of the leaf contain vascular bundles. Each vascular bundle consists of xylem and phloem. Xylem is usually present towards the upper surface and conducts water and minerals. Phloem is present towards the lower surface and conducts prepared food.

Vascular bundles are often surrounded by a bundle sheath, which gives support and helps in transport.

4. Dicot Leaf Anatomy

A typical dicot leaf is dorsiventral, meaning the upper and lower surfaces are different. The upper epidermis has a cuticle and fewer stomata, while the lower epidermis has more stomata.

The mesophyll is differentiated into palisade and spongy parenchyma. Vascular bundles are arranged in a network according to reticulate venation.

Example: Sunflower leaf.

5. Monocot Leaf Anatomy

A typical monocot leaf is usually isobilateral, meaning both surfaces are almost similar. Stomata are present on both epidermal surfaces.

The mesophyll is generally undifferentiated. Vascular bundles are numerous and arranged parallel to each other. In grasses, large bulliform cells are present in the upper epidermis. These cells help in rolling and unrolling of leaves during water stress.

Example: Maize leaf.

6. Xerophytic Leaf Anatomy

Xerophytic leaves are adapted to dry conditions. They usually have a thick cuticle, sunken stomata, compact mesophyll and well-developed mechanical tissues.

These features reduce water loss and help the plant survive in dry habitats.

Example: Nerium.

Conclusion: The leaf is internally organized into epidermis, mesophyll and vascular bundles. The epidermis protects the leaf, mesophyll performs photosynthesis, and vascular bundles conduct water and food. Dicot leaves are dorsiventral, monocot leaves are isobilateral, and xerophytic leaves show special adaptations to reduce water loss. Thus, leaf anatomy makes the leaf suitable for photosynthesis, transpiration and gaseous exchange.

Anatomy of Primary Monocot and Dicot Stems and Roots

The primary structure of stems and roots is formed by the activity of the apical meristem. In both stems and roots, the primary body is made up of three tissue systems: epidermal tissue system, ground tissue system and vascular tissue system. Monocots and dicots show clear anatomical differences in the arrangement of vascular bundles, presence or absence of cambium, and the possibility of secondary growth.

1. Anatomy of Primary Dicot Stem

A typical dicot stem, such as sunflower stem, shows a well-organized internal structure with vascular bundles arranged in a ring.

Epidermis

The epidermis is the outermost single layer of compactly arranged cells. It is covered externally by a cuticle and may bear multicellular hairs. It protects the internal tissues and reduces water loss.

Cortex

The cortex lies below the epidermis and is usually differentiated into three regions: hypodermis, general cortex and endodermis. The hypodermis is generally collenchymatous and provides mechanical support. The general cortex is parenchymatous and stores food. The innermost layer of cortex is the endodermis, often called the starch sheath because it may contain starch grains.

Pericycle

The pericycle lies inner to the endodermis. In many dicot stems, it occurs as patches of sclerenchymatous fibres above the vascular bundles and gives additional mechanical support.

Vascular bundles

The vascular bundles are arranged in a ring around the pith. Each vascular bundle is conjoint, collateral, open and endarch. It is conjoint because xylem and phloem occur in the same bundle, collateral because phloem lies outside and xylem inside, open because cambium is present between xylem and phloem, and endarch because protoxylem lies towards the centre. The presence of cambium allows secondary growth.

Pith and medullary rays

The central region is occupied by a large parenchymatous pith. Between vascular bundles, medullary rays are present and help in radial conduction of food and water.

2. Anatomy of Primary Monocot Stem

A typical monocot stem, such as maize stem, differs from dicot stem mainly in having scattered vascular bundles and undifferentiated ground tissue.

Epidermis

The epidermis is the outermost protective layer. It is single-layered and covered by a cuticle.

Ground tissue

In monocot stem, the ground tissue is not differentiated into cortex, endodermis, pericycle and pith. It is mainly parenchymatous and fills the entire inner region of the stem.

Vascular bundles

The vascular bundles are numerous and scattered throughout the ground tissue. They are more crowded towards the periphery and fewer towards the centre. Each vascular bundle is conjoint, collateral, closed and endarch.

The vascular bundle is closed because cambium is absent between xylem and phloem. Due to absence of cambium, normal secondary growth does not occur in most monocot stems.

Each vascular bundle is surrounded by a sclerenchymatous bundle sheath. The xylem usually shows metaxylem vessels and protoxylem elements. In maize, the protoxylem disintegrates and forms a protoxylem lacuna, which is a characteristic feature of monocot stem.

3. Anatomy of Primary Dicot Root

A typical dicot root, such as gram root, shows radial vascular arrangement, exarch xylem and small or absent pith.

Epiblema

The outermost layer is called epiblema or piliferous layer. It is thin-walled and bears unicellular root hairs, which increase the surface area for absorption of water and minerals. Cuticle is absent because the root is mainly concerned with absorption.

Cortex

The cortex is broad and made of parenchymatous cells with intercellular spaces. It stores food and helps in movement of water from epiblema to inner tissues.

Endodermis

The innermost layer of the cortex is the endodermis. It is made of barrel-shaped cells. The radial and transverse walls have Casparian strips made of suberin. The endodermis regulates the movement of water and minerals into the stele.

Pericycle

The pericycle lies just inner to the endodermis. It is usually single-layered and parenchymatous. It gives rise to lateral roots. In dicot roots, it also contributes to formation of vascular cambium and cork cambium during secondary growth.

Vascular tissue

Xylem and phloem are arranged alternately on different radii, so the vascular bundle is radial. The xylem is exarch, meaning protoxylem lies towards the periphery and metaxylem towards the centre. The number of xylem groups is usually limited, commonly diarch, triarch, tetrarch or pentarch. The pith is very small or absent. Secondary growth usually occurs in dicot roots.

4. Anatomy of Primary Monocot Root

A typical monocot root, such as maize root, resembles dicot root in having radial vascular bundles and exarch xylem, but differs in having many xylem groups and a large pith.

Epiblema

The epiblema is the outermost layer and bears unicellular root hairs. It helps in absorption of water and minerals. Cuticle is absent.

Cortex

The cortex is broad and parenchymatous. It has intercellular spaces and helps in storage and conduction of water.

Endodermis

The endodermis is distinct and has Casparian strips. Some thin-walled passage cells may be present opposite the protoxylem. These cells help in the passage of water into the stele.

Pericycle

The pericycle lies inner to the endodermis. It gives rise to lateral roots. However, it does not usually take part in secondary growth because normal secondary growth is absent in monocot roots.

Vascular tissue

Xylem and phloem are arranged alternately in a radial manner. The xylem is exarch. The number of xylem bundles is many, so the condition is called polyarch. A large and well-developed parenchymatous pith is present in the centre. This is an important difference from dicot root.

These anatomical features are very important for identifying monocot and dicot plants and understanding their structural organization.

Important Parts of Secondary Growth

Secondary growth increases the thickness of stems and roots due to the activity of lateral meristems. The vascular cambium produces secondary xylem and secondary phloem, while cork cambium forms protective tissues outside the stem.

Spring Wood and Autumn Wood

In the spring season, cambium is very active and produces a large number of xylary elements with wider vessels and wider cavities. The wood formed during this season is called spring wood or early wood.

In winter or unfavourable season, cambium is less active and forms fewer xylary elements with narrow vessels. This wood is called autumn wood or late wood.

Together, spring wood and autumn wood of one year form an annual ring. Annual rings are useful in estimating the age of woody plants and studying past environmental conditions.

Sapwood and Heartwood

The young elements of secondary xylem present towards the peripheral region form sapwood or alburnum. Sapwood is active in the conduction of water and minerals from root to leaf.

With age, the central region of sapwood becomes converted into heartwood or duramen. This happens due to deposition of organic compounds such as tannins, resins, oils, gums, aromatic substances and essential oils in the inner layers of the stem.

Heartwood does not conduct water, but it gives strong mechanical support to the stem and makes the wood darker, harder and more durable.

Formation of Cork Cambium

A secondary meristem called cork cambium or phellogen develops usually in the cortex region. It is made of narrow, thin-walled and nearly rectangular cells.

Phellogen cuts off cells on both sides. The outer cells differentiate into cork or phellem, while the inner cells differentiate into secondary cortex or phelloderm.

Phellogen, phellem and phelloderm together form the periderm. Cork is impervious to water because of suberin deposition in its cell walls, while phelloderm is usually parenchymatous.

Bark

During secondary growth, the tissues present outside the vascular cambium are generally included in the bark. It may include secondary phloem, cortex remnants and periderm, depending on the age of the stem.

Bark protects the inner living tissues from mechanical injury, water loss, temperature changes and microbial attack. In older stems, repeated formation of cork cambium produces a thick protective bark.

Lenticels

At certain regions, the phellogen cuts off loosely arranged parenchymatous cells on the outer side instead of compact cork cells. These cells rupture the epidermis and form lens-shaped openings called lenticels.

Lenticels permit the exchange of gases between the outer atmosphere and the internal tissues of the stem. They occur commonly in woody trees.

Anomalous Structure in the Primary Body of Stem

The normal primary structure of a stem shows epidermis, cortex, vascular bundles and pith. In a typical dicot stem, vascular bundles are arranged in a ring and are conjoint, collateral, open and endarch. In a typical monocot stem, vascular bundles are scattered and closed. However, in some plants, the primary structure of the stem deviates from the normal pattern. Such deviations are called anomalous structures.

Meaning of Anomalous Primary Structure

An anomalous primary structure is a condition in which the primary tissues of the stem do not follow the usual anatomical plan. The abnormality may be seen in the vascular system, cortex, pith or arrangement of vascular bundles.

In most cases, anomalous primary structure of stem is related to the position, arrangement and nature of vascular bundles. It should not be confused with anomalous secondary growth, which occurs due to unusual cambial activity after primary growth.

Causes of Anomalous Structure in Primary Stem

  1. Unusual arrangement of vascular bundles.
  2. Presence of extra vascular bundles in cortex or pith.
  3. Development of bicollateral vascular bundles.
  4. Scattered arrangement of vascular bundles.
  5. Presence of amphivasal or concentric vascular bundles.
  6. Adaptation for special functions such as storage, climbing, support or conduction.
04

Types of Anomalous Structure in Primary Stem

1. Scattered vascular bundles

In a typical dicot stem, vascular bundles are arranged in a ring. However, in monocot stems, vascular bundles are scattered throughout the ground tissue. This scattered arrangement is considered anomalous when compared with the normal dicot plan.

In monocot stems such as maize, the vascular bundles are numerous and scattered in the ground tissue. They are more crowded towards the periphery and fewer towards the centre. Each vascular bundle is closed because cambium is absent, and a sclerenchymatous bundle sheath surrounds each bundle.

This type of arrangement gives mechanical strength and is suitable for monocot stems where secondary growth is usually absent.

Example: Zea mays.

2. Cortical vascular bundles

In a normal stem, vascular bundles are present in the stele. But in some plants, additional vascular bundles are present in the cortex. These are called cortical vascular bundles.

Cortical bundles occur outside the normal ring of vascular bundles. They may help in additional conduction and support. Such bundles are commonly found in some members of families like Cucurbitaceae and Nyctaginaceae.

In Cucurbita, cortical vascular bundles may occur in the cortex along with the normal vascular bundles. These extra bundles help in supplying water and food to the large, rapidly growing stem.

Example: Cucurbita.

3. Medullary vascular bundles

When vascular bundles occur in the pith region, they are called medullary vascular bundles. In a normal dicot stem, the pith is parenchymatous and does not contain vascular bundles. Therefore, the presence of vascular bundles in the pith is an anomalous condition.

Medullary bundles are found in plants such as Amaranthus, Boerhaavia and some members of Chenopodiaceae. These bundles are usually additional to the normal vascular ring.

They help in increasing the conducting capacity of the stem and may also provide support.

Examples: Amaranthus, Boerhaavia.

4. Bicollateral vascular bundles

In a normal collateral vascular bundle, phloem is present only on the outer side of xylem. But in a bicollateral vascular bundle, phloem is present on both outer and inner sides of the xylem.

A bicollateral bundle has the following arrangement from outside to inside:

Outer phloem → outer cambium → xylem → inner cambium → inner phloem

This type of bundle is considered anomalous because the presence of internal phloem is not common in ordinary dicot stems. Bicollateral bundles are commonly found in members of Cucurbitaceae and Solanaceae. In Cucurbita, the vascular bundles are bicollateral and arranged in two rings.

Examples: Cucurbita, Datura.

5. Amphivasal or leptocentric vascular bundles

In a normal vascular bundle, xylem and phloem occur side by side. In an amphivasal vascular bundle, the xylem surrounds the phloem. Such a vascular bundle is also called leptocentric.

This type of bundle is unusual in ordinary stems and is therefore included under anomalous primary structure. It is found in some monocot stems and rhizomes.

Examples: Dracaena, Yucca.

6. Amphicribral or hadrocentric vascular bundles

In an amphicribral vascular bundle, the phloem surrounds the xylem. This type of bundle is also called hadrocentric. It is more common in some ferns, but may also occur in certain plant organs.

It is anomalous because it differs from the normal collateral vascular bundle arrangement of flowering plant stems.

7. Successive rings of vascular bundles in the primary body

In some stems, vascular bundles may occur in more than one ring even in the primary structure. In a typical dicot stem, only one ring of vascular bundles is expected. The presence of additional rings or irregular rings of bundles is considered anomalous.

This condition helps in extra conduction and mechanical support, especially in plants with special habits.

Examples: some species of Amaranthus and Boerhaavia.

Significance of Anomalous Primary Structure

Anomalous primary structures are important because they show that plant anatomy is not always fixed. Different plants may modify their internal structure according to habit, habitat and physiological needs.

Cortical and medullary bundles increase the conducting capacity of the stem. Bicollateral bundles provide additional phloem for food conduction. Scattered bundles in monocots provide support in the absence of normal secondary growth. Concentric bundles such as amphivasal and amphicribral bundles show special vascular organization.

These features are also useful in plant identification and classification.

Conclusion

Anomalous structure in the primary body of stem refers to deviations from the normal arrangement of primary tissues, especially vascular bundles. It includes scattered vascular bundles, cortical vascular bundles, medullary vascular bundles, bicollateral bundles and concentric bundles such as amphivasal and amphicribral bundles.

These anomalies are important because they increase conduction, provide support and help plants adapt to special habits. They are also useful anatomical characters for identification and classification.
