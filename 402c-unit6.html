Secondary Anomalous Growth in Dicot and Monocot Stems

With special reference to Bignonia, Tecoma, Amaranthus and Dracaena. This study sheet explains the meaning of anomalous secondary growth, its causes, major examples in dicot and monocot stems, and a compact comparison for exam revision.

1. Introduction and Meaning 2. Dicot Examples: Bignonia, Tecoma, Amaranthus 3. Monocot Example: Dracaena 4. Comparison, Significance and Conclusion
01

Introduction and Meaning of Anomalous Secondary Growth

Secondary growth is the increase in thickness of stem and root due to the activity of lateral meristems, mainly vascular cambium and cork cambium. In a normal dicot stem, a continuous vascular cambium ring is formed. This cambium cuts off secondary xylem towards the inner side and secondary phloem towards the outer side, producing a regular woody cylinder. However, in some plants, secondary growth does not follow the normal pattern. The cambium may behave unequally, extra cambia may arise, phloem may become included within xylem, or a special secondary thickening meristem may develop. Such unusual secondary growth is called anomalous secondary growth.

General introduction

Anomalous secondary growth is commonly seen in some dicot stems such as Bignonia, Tecoma and Amaranthus. It is also seen in some monocot stems such as Dracaena, where normal vascular cambium is absent.

These anomalies are important because they modify the normal arrangement of secondary xylem, secondary phloem and associated tissues. They often help the plant in climbing, conduction, storage, flexibility or mechanical support.

Meaning of anomalous secondary growth

Anomalous secondary growth is the abnormal increase in thickness of a stem due to irregular cambial activity or the development of additional cambia. It results in unusual arrangement of secondary xylem, secondary phloem and other tissues.

It may occur due to:

  1. Unequal activity of the normal cambium.
  2. Formation of phloem wedges or included phloem.
  3. Formation of successive cambial rings.
  4. Development of a special secondary thickening meristem in monocots.
  5. Adaptation to climbing habit, storage, conduction or mechanical support.
02

Anomalous Secondary Growth in Dicot Stems

1. Anomalous Secondary Growth in Bignonia

General account

Bignonia is a woody climber belonging to Bignoniaceae. It shows anomalous secondary growth due to unequal activity of the vascular cambium. The main feature is the formation of wedge-shaped masses of phloem inside the secondary xylem. These are called phloem wedges.

Young stem structure

The young stem of Bignonia is more or less like a normal dicot stem. It has epidermis, cortex, pericycle, vascular bundles and pith. The vascular bundles are arranged in a ring and are conjoint, collateral and open. The fascicular cambium is present between xylem and phloem. Later, interfascicular cambium develops between the vascular bundles and forms a complete cambial ring.

Normal secondary growth at first

Initially, the cambium behaves normally. It produces secondary xylem towards the inner side and secondary phloem towards the outer side. Thus, a normal secondary vascular cylinder begins to form.

Unequal cambial activity

After some time, the cambium becomes unequal in activity. In four regions, the cambium produces more secondary phloem and less secondary xylem. In the remaining regions, it continues to produce more secondary xylem in the usual way.

Because of this unequal activity, wedge-shaped masses of phloem are formed and pushed inward into the secondary xylem.

Formation of phloem wedges

The phloem wedges are the most characteristic feature of Bignonia. Usually, four phloem wedges are formed. These wedges extend from the outer phloem region towards the centre and interrupt the continuity of the secondary xylem.

As a result, the secondary xylem appears divided into four arms. In transverse section, the stem often shows a cross-like appearance.

Final structure

The mature stem of Bignonia shows a central pith, secondary xylem divided into arms, and wedge-shaped phloem masses embedded within the wood. The normal outer phloem is also present. This structure gives strength as well as flexibility.

Significance

This anomalous growth is useful for the climbing habit of Bignonia. A climber needs a stem that is strong but not too rigid. The phloem wedges interrupt the solid woody cylinder and provide flexibility, allowing the stem to twist and climb without breaking.

2. Anomalous Secondary Growth in Tecoma

General account

Tecoma is also a member of Bignoniaceae and shows anomalous secondary growth similar to Bignonia. The main anomaly is the formation of included phloem or interxylary phloem due to abnormal cambial activity.

Young stem structure

The young stem has a typical dicot structure. The vascular bundles are arranged in a ring. Each bundle is conjoint, collateral and open. Fascicular and interfascicular cambia unite to form a complete cambial ring.

Beginning of secondary growth

At first, the cambium behaves normally and forms secondary xylem inside and secondary phloem outside. A normal secondary vascular cylinder is produced in the early stage.

Abnormal cambial activity

Later, certain parts of the cambium become abnormal in function. These cambial regions produce more phloem instead of normal xylem. As a result, patches or wedges of phloem become embedded within the secondary xylem.

This phloem present inside the xylem is called included phloem or interxylary phloem.

Formation of included phloem

The included phloem occurs as patches or wedge-shaped masses within the secondary xylem. The xylem cylinder becomes interrupted by these softer phloem areas. Thus, the wood does not remain a uniform continuous cylinder.

Final structure

In transverse section, the stem of Tecoma shows secondary xylem interrupted by included phloem. The phloem occurs not only outside the xylem, as in normal dicot stems, but also inside the xylem region.

Significance

The presence of included phloem increases the amount of food-conducting tissue. It also makes the stem more flexible. This is useful in weak-stemmed or climbing plants where the stem must bend without breaking.

3. Anomalous Secondary Growth in Amaranthus

General account

Amaranthus shows anomalous secondary growth due to the formation of successive cambia. Unlike a normal dicot stem, where only one cambial ring is active, Amaranthus produces additional cambial rings one after another.

Young stem structure

The young stem of Amaranthus shows a typical dicot arrangement in the beginning. It has epidermis, cortex, pericycle, vascular bundles and pith. The primary vascular bundles are arranged in a ring. A normal cambium may develop and function for a short time.

Formation of first cambium

The first cambium produces secondary xylem towards the inner side and secondary phloem towards the outer side. This resembles normal secondary growth.

Formation of successive cambia

After the activity of the first cambium, new cambial rings arise outside the previous vascular tissue. These cambia usually originate from parenchymatous cells outside the vascular region, such as pericyclic or conjunctive tissue.

Each newly formed cambium produces secondary vascular bundles. It cuts off xylem towards the inner side and phloem towards the outer side. In between the vascular bundles, conjunctive tissue is also formed.

Resulting structure

Due to the formation of successive cambia, several rings or irregular groups of vascular bundles are formed. These vascular bundles remain embedded in conjunctive tissue. The stem therefore does not show a single regular ring of secondary xylem and phloem like an ordinary dicot stem.

The mature stem of Amaranthus shows multiple rings or scattered groups of secondary vascular bundles formed by successive cambia.

Significance

This type of anomalous secondary growth increases the thickness of the stem and improves conduction. It is common in some members of Amaranthaceae and related families. The formation of successive cambia provides additional vascular tissue and helps the plant maintain strength and transport capacity.

Quick dicot revision

  • Bignonia: unequal cambial activity → phloem wedges inside secondary xylem.
  • Tecoma: abnormal cambium → included or interxylary phloem embedded in xylem.
  • Amaranthus: successive cambial rings → multiple rings/groups of vascular bundles.

These three dicot examples show that anomalous secondary growth may arise either from irregular behavior of the normal cambium or from the appearance of additional cambia.

Anomalous Secondary Growth in the Monocot Stem Dracaena

4. Anomalous Secondary Growth in Dracaena

General account

Dracaena is a monocot plant. Most monocots do not show normal secondary growth because they lack normal vascular cambium. However, Dracaena shows secondary thickening by means of a special meristem called the secondary thickening meristem.

This type of growth is anomalous because it is different from normal dicot secondary growth.

Primary structure

The young stem of Dracaena has scattered vascular bundles, like a typical monocot stem. The vascular bundles are closed, meaning cambium is absent. Therefore, normal secondary growth cannot occur.

Origin of secondary thickening meristem

A special meristematic zone develops outside the primary vascular bundles, usually in the outer region of the ground tissue. This is called the secondary thickening meristem.

This meristem is not the same as the normal vascular cambium of dicots.

Activity of secondary thickening meristem

The secondary thickening meristem cuts off cells mainly towards the inner side. These cells differentiate into secondary ground tissue and secondary vascular bundles. Towards the outer side, it may produce a small amount of parenchymatous tissue.

The vascular bundles produced by this meristem are usually concentric amphivasal bundles. In amphivasal bundles, xylem surrounds the phloem.

Final structure

The mature stem of Dracaena shows many secondary vascular bundles embedded in secondary ground tissue. These bundles are not arranged in a continuous ring like dicot secondary xylem. Instead, they are scattered in the secondary tissue.

Because of this secondary thickening, Dracaena can become tree-like even though it is a monocot.

Significance

The anomalous secondary growth of Dracaena is important because it allows increase in girth in a monocot stem. Since monocots usually lack normal vascular cambium, this special secondary thickening meristem helps in mechanical support and conduction. It enables plants like Dracaena and Yucca to develop woody, tree-like stems.

Monocot revision point

In monocots, normal vascular cambium is absent. Therefore, anomalous secondary growth, when present, depends on a special secondary thickening meristem rather than on the ordinary vascular cambium.

The vascular bundles formed secondarily in Dracaena are commonly amphivasal, and they become embedded in newly formed ground tissue. This provides extra conduction and support and permits girth increase.

Significance of anomalous secondary growth

Anomalous secondary growth is important because it allows plants to modify their internal structure according to their habit and need. In climbers like Bignonia and Tecoma, the interruption of wood by phloem wedges or included phloem provides flexibility. In Amaranthus, successive cambia produce additional vascular bundles and increase conducting capacity. In Dracaena, secondary thickening allows a monocot plant to grow thick and tree-like despite the absence of normal cambium.

Thus, anomalous secondary growth helps in mechanical support, conduction, storage and adaptation to special habits.

Conclusion

Anomalous secondary growth is an unusual form of secondary thickening caused by irregular cambial activity or formation of additional meristems. In Bignonia, unequal cambial activity forms phloem wedges. In Tecoma, included or interxylary phloem is formed within the secondary xylem. In Amaranthus, successive cambia produce additional rings or groups of vascular bundles. In Dracaena, a monocot stem thickens by the activity of a secondary thickening meristem which forms secondary amphivasal bundles.

These examples show that secondary growth in plants can vary greatly according to structure, habit and functional requirements.
