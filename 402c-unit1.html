Topic 1 · Updated answer
01

Theories Regarding the Origin of Angiosperms

Angiosperms are the most advanced and dominant group of seed plants. They are characterized by true flowers, enclosed ovules, fruits, double fertilization and endosperm formation. Their origin is one of the most debated problems in botany because angiosperms appear rather suddenly in the fossil record and became dominant during the Cretaceous period.

Several theories have been proposed to explain the origin of angiosperms and their flowers. Important theories include the Euanthial or Anthostrobilus theory, Pseudanthium theory, Bennettitalean or Cycadeoidales theory, Caytoniaceae theory, Cycadales theory, Anthocorm theory, Gonophyll theory and Herbaceous origin theory.

Exam focus For B.Sc. answers, write the theory, its main idea, important support and limitation. End with the view that no single theory fully explains the origin of angiosperms.
Origin theories
02

Major Theories of Origin of Angiosperms

1. Euanthial or Anthostrobilus Theory

The Euanthial theory is one of the most important theories regarding the origin of the angiosperm flower. It was supported by botanists such as Arber and Parkin.

According to this theory, the angiosperm flower is a single modified bisexual strobilus. The primitive flower was large, bisexual, radially symmetrical and had many free floral parts arranged spirally on an elongated axis. Such a flower resembles primitive angiosperm flowers such as Magnolia.

This theory suggests that stamens and carpels evolved from microsporophylls and megasporophylls of gymnosperm-like ancestors. The carpel evolved by folding and fusion of a megasporophyll, enclosing the ovules within an ovary.

Merit: It explains the origin of primitive bisexual flowers with many free parts. Demerit: It does not fully explain unisexual and highly reduced flowers.

2. Pseudanthium Theory

The Pseudanthium theory was proposed mainly by Wettstein. According to this theory, the angiosperm flower did not originate as a single bisexual strobilus. Instead, it originated from a compact inflorescence of unisexual flowers.

The word pseudanthium means “false flower”. According to this view, what appears to be a single flower was actually a condensed cluster of many small unisexual flowers. Male flowers gave rise to stamens, while female flowers gave rise to carpels.

This theory considers the primitive angiosperm flower to be small, unisexual, simple and often wind-pollinated. Plants such as Casuarina, Piper and some Amentiferae were considered primitive by supporters of this view.

Merit: It explains reduced, unisexual and wind-pollinated flowers. Demerit: It does not satisfactorily explain primitive bisexual flowers like Magnolia.

3. Bennettitalean or Cycadeoidales Theory

The Bennettitalean theory proposes that angiosperms originated from Bennettitales, also called Cycadeoidales. Bennettitales were extinct gymnosperms of the Mesozoic era. Their reproductive structures were often flower-like and sometimes bisexual.

In genera such as Cycadeoidea and Williamsonia, the reproductive organs showed a central receptacle bearing ovules and surrounding microsporophylls or protective bracts. This flower-like organization suggested to some botanists that angiosperm flowers evolved from Bennettitalean reproductive structures.

Merit: The theory is attractive because Bennettitales had flower-like reproductive structures. Demerit: Their ovules were not enclosed inside true carpels, so they are not considered direct ancestors of angiosperms.

4. Caytoniaceae Theory

The Caytoniaceae theory suggests that angiosperms may have originated from the fossil seed plant group Caytoniales or Caytoniaceae. Important fossil members include Caytonia.

Caytoniales had seed-bearing structures in which ovules were enclosed within cupule-like organs. These cupules were partly closed structures and were considered by some botanists as possible precursors of the angiosperm carpel.

According to this theory, the closed carpel of angiosperms may have evolved from a folded or closed cupule-like structure. This theory is important because it tries to explain the origin of one of the most characteristic features of angiosperms: the enclosed ovule.

Merit: It gives a possible explanation for the origin of the closed carpel. Demerit: The cupule of Caytoniales is not identical with the true carpel of angiosperms.

5. Cycadales Theory

The Cycadales theory suggests that angiosperms may have originated from cycad-like gymnosperms. Cycads are primitive gymnosperms with large pinnate leaves, large ovules and simple reproductive structures.

This theory is based on the resemblance between primitive angiosperms and cycads in some vegetative and reproductive features. For example, both may show large leaves, large ovules and woody habit. Some primitive angiosperms also show cycad-like features in their large floral organs.

Merit: Cycads have primitive seed-plant characters comparable with early angiosperms, especially in megasporophylls. Demerit: Cycads do not have true flowers, enclosed ovules or fruits; therefore, they are not considered direct ancestors of angiosperms.

6. Anthocorm Theory

The Anthocorm theory was proposed to explain the origin of angiosperm flowers from a branched reproductive system rather than from a single flower. According to this theory, the primitive angiosperm reproductive structure was an anthocorm, meaning a flower-bearing branch system.

In this view, the angiosperm flower or floral shoot evolved from a complex branching system bearing reproductive organs. Through reduction, condensation and fusion, the reproductive branch system became transformed into a compact flower.

Merit: It explains the flower as part of a modified shoot system and helps in understanding the relationship between inflorescence and flower. Demerit: It is complex and less directly supported by fossil evidence.

7. Gonophyll Theory

The Gonophyll theory explains the origin of reproductive organs of angiosperms from fertile leaf-like structures called gonophylls. A gonophyll is a modified leaf or leaf-like organ bearing reproductive structures.

According to this theory, stamens and carpels originated from fertile leaves. Stamens are interpreted as modified microsporophylls, and carpels as modified megasporophylls. The carpel may have evolved by folding of a megasporophyll so that the ovules became enclosed inside.

Merit: It gives a simple explanation of the foliar nature of stamens and carpels. Demerit: It does not fully explain highly fused or syncarpous gynoecia and the complete origin of the angiosperm flower.

8. Herbaceous Origin Theory

The Herbaceous origin theory suggests that the earliest angiosperms were not large woody trees, but small herbaceous plants. According to this view, herbaceous habit may be primitive in angiosperms.

Supporters of this theory argue that herbs have shorter life cycles, faster reproduction and greater adaptability. These features could have helped early angiosperms diversify rapidly during the Cretaceous period. Small herbaceous plants could colonize disturbed habitats more easily than large trees.

Merit: It explains the rapid spread and diversification of angiosperms. Demerit: Many primitive angiosperms show woody characters, so this theory is not accepted as a complete explanation.

ConclusionThe origin of angiosperms is still a debated topic. The Euanthial theory considers the flower as a single bisexual strobilus, while the Pseudanthium theory considers it as a condensed inflorescence. The Bennettitalean, Caytoniaceae and Cycadales theories trace angiosperms from different gymnospermous ancestors. The Anthocorm and Gonophyll theories explain the flower and floral organs as modified shoot or leaf systems. The Herbaceous origin theory suggests that early angiosperms may have been small, fast-growing herbs. No single theory fully explains angiosperm origin, but together these theories help us understand the possible evolutionary pathways that led to flowering plants.
Evolutionary trends
03

Evolution of Angiosperms

1. Evolution of flower

The flower is a highly modified reproductive shoot bearing sepals, petals, stamens and carpels. Primitive flowers were probably bisexual and radially symmetrical; later flowers became more specialized with reduced parts, fused floral organs and bilateral symmetry.

2. Carpel and enclosed ovule

The carpel encloses the ovules inside the ovary. This protected ovules from desiccation, injury and herbivores and allowed specialized pollination mechanisms.

3. Double fertilization

Angiosperms show double fertilization. One male gamete fuses with the egg to form zygote, while the other fuses with polar nuclei to form endosperm. This prevents wastage of food tissue.

4. Fruit

After fertilization, the ovary develops into a fruit. Fruits protect seeds and help in dispersal by wind, water, animals or mechanical means.

5. Vessels and sieve tubes

Angiosperms developed efficient vascular tissues. Xylem has vessels, while phloem has sieve tubes and companion cells, allowing efficient conduction.

6. Pollination mechanisms

Angiosperms evolved wind, water, insect, bird and animal pollination. Insect pollination was especially important in rapid diversification of flowering plants.

7. Habit and habitat

Early angiosperms were probably small trees or shrubs. Later they evolved into herbs, climbers, aquatic plants, epiphytes, parasites and desert plants.

Causes of successAngiosperms became dominant because of flowers, enclosed ovules, fruits, double fertilization, efficient vascular tissues, reduced gametophytes, insect pollination and wide adaptability.
Topic 2 · Flowering shoot
04

Inflorescence

In angiosperms, the flower is the main reproductive structure. Flowers may occur singly or in groups. When flowers are arranged in a definite pattern on a floral axis, the arrangement is called an inflorescence. The floral axis is called the peduncle.

1. Racemose inflorescence

The main axis continues to grow indefinitely and does not end in a flower. Flowers are produced laterally in acropetal succession, with older flowers at the base and younger flowers towards the apex. Examples include mustard, radish, sunflower and coriander. Types include raceme, spike, catkin, spadix, corymb, umbel and capitulum.

2. Cymose inflorescence

The main axis has limited growth and ends in a flower. Flowers are produced in basipetal succession, with older flowers at the apex and younger flowers towards the base. Examples include jasmine, hibiscus, bougainvillea and cotton. Types include monochasial, dichasial and polychasial cyme.

3. Special types

In cyathium, as in Euphorbia, the inflorescence resembles a single flower. In hypanthodium, as in Ficus, flowers are enclosed inside a hollow fleshy receptacle. In verticillaster, as in Ocimum and Salvia, flowers occur in false whorls at nodes.

Angiospermic flower
05

Angiospermic Flower

A typical angiospermic flower is a specialized reproductive structure borne on a stalk called the pedicel. The swollen tip of the pedicel is called the thalamus or receptacle. On the thalamus, floral parts are arranged in four whorls: calyx, corolla, androecium and gynoecium.

Calyx

The outermost whorl, made of sepals. Sepals are usually green and protect the flower in bud condition.

Corolla

Made of petals. Petals are often brightly coloured and help in attracting pollinators.

Androecium

The male reproductive whorl made of stamens. Each stamen has a filament and anther, and the anther produces pollen grains.

Gynoecium

The female reproductive whorl made of one or more carpels. A carpel has stigma, style and ovary. Ovary contains ovules.

Important featuresA flower may be complete or incomplete, bisexual or unisexual, actinomorphic or zygomorphic, and hypogynous, perigynous or epigynous.
Concept answer
06

Flower as a Modified Determinate Shoot

A flower is considered a modified determinate shoot because it shows many similarities with a vegetative shoot but is modified for reproduction. A shoot has an axis with nodes, internodes and leaves. Similarly, a flower has a short floral axis called the thalamus, on which floral leaves are arranged.

Sepals, petals, stamens and carpels are regarded as modified leaves. Sepals are protective leaves, petals are attractive leaves, stamens are microsporophylls and carpels are megasporophylls. The flower is determinate because its growth is limited and stops after floral organs are formed.

Evidence 1

Floral parts are arranged on the thalamus in a definite order, just as leaves are arranged on a stem.

Evidence 2

Sepals are often green and leaf-like. Stamens and carpels are highly modified sporophylls.

Evidence 3

In abnormal flowers, floral organs may become leaf-like. This condition is called phyllody.

Evidence 4

The vascular supply of floral organs resembles the vascular supply of leaves.

Evidence 5

The flower arises from a bud like a vegetative shoot, but its internodes are condensed and growth is limited.

Topic 3 · Fruit types
07

Special Types of Fruits

A fruit is a mature or ripened ovary formed after fertilization. In true fruits, the ovary alone forms the fruit. In some plants, other floral parts such as thalamus, calyx, bracts or inflorescence axis also take part in fruit formation. Such fruits are called special fruits.

1. Spurious Fruits or False Fruits

A spurious fruit, also called a false fruit or pseudocarp, is a fruit in which the edible or fleshy part develops not only from the ovary but also from other floral parts such as thalamus, receptacle, calyx or bracts.

Example: Dillenia

In Dillenia, the true fruits are formed from the carpels, but they are enclosed by thick, fleshy and persistent sepals. The fleshy sepals form the edible portion and surround the actual fruit. Therefore, Dillenia is a spurious fruit.

Aggregate fruits
08

Aggregate Fruits

An aggregate fruit is formed from a single flower having many free carpels. Such a flower is called apocarpous. Each carpel develops into a small fruitlet, and all fruitlets remain grouped together on the same thalamus.

Annona

In custard apple, many free carpels develop into berry-like fruitlets that become crowded and fused on a common thalamus. It is an etaerio of berries.

Michelia

The apocarpous gynoecium has many free carpels arranged on an elongated thalamus. Each carpel develops into a follicle. It is an etaerio of follicles.

Catharanthus

The gynoecium consists of two free carpels. After fertilization, each carpel develops into a follicle, forming a pair of follicles.

Polyalthia

Many free carpels develop into separate fruitlets, usually berry-like or monocarpellary, borne together on the same thalamus.

Multiple fruits
09

Multiple Fruits or Composite Fruits

A multiple fruit is formed from the whole inflorescence. Many flowers of an inflorescence take part in forming a single fruit-like structure.

Ananas

In pineapple, the fruit develops from a spike inflorescence. The ovaries, bracts, perianth and inflorescence axis become fleshy and fuse together. It is a multiple fruit of sorosis type.

Artocarpus

In jackfruit, the fruit develops from a female inflorescence. The perianth and other floral parts become fleshy and fuse together. The edible bulbs represent the fleshy perianth of individual flowers. It is also a sorosis.

Topic 4 · Morphology
10

Homology of Plant Parts

Homology is the relationship between plant organs that have the same origin and basic structure, but may perform different functions. In simple words, homologous organs are similar in origin but different in function.

Leaf tendril and leaf spine

In Pisum, leaflets are modified into tendrils. In Opuntia and Berberis, leaves are modified into spines. Their functions differ but origin is leaf.

Stem tendril and stem thorn

In Cucurbita and Vitis, stems are modified into tendrils. In Citrus and Bougainvillea, axillary branches are modified into thorns. Both are stem in origin.

Rhizome, tuber and corm

Rhizome in ginger, tuber in potato and corm in Colocasia are all underground stem modifications.

Floral parts

Sepals, petals, stamens and carpels are regarded as modified leaves. They differ in function but have leaf origin.

Phylloclade and cladode

In Opuntia, the stem forms a phylloclade. In Asparagus, branches form cladodes. Both are stem modifications.

Analogy
11

Analogy of Plant Parts

Analogy is the relationship between plant organs that perform the same function but have different origin and structure. In simple words, analogous organs are similar in function but different in origin.

Leaf tendril and stem tendril

In Pisum, the tendril is leaf in origin. In Cucurbita and Vitis, the tendril is stem or branch in origin. Both help in climbing.

Leaf spine and stem thorn

In Opuntia or Berberis, spines are leaf in origin. In Citrus and Bougainvillea, thorns are stem in origin. Both protect the plant.

Phylloclade and foliage leaf

A phylloclade of Opuntia and a normal leaf both perform photosynthesis, but one is stem in origin and the other is leaf in origin.

Cladode and leaf

A cladode of Asparagus and a normal leaf both perform photosynthesis, but differ in origin.

Root tuber and stem tuber

Sweet potato is a root tuber and potato is a stem tuber. Both store food, but their origin is different.

ConclusionHomology and analogy are important concepts in plant morphology. Homologous organs have the same origin but may perform different functions, while analogous organs have different origin but perform similar functions. Homology explains divergent evolution, while analogy explains convergent evolution.
