Topic 1
01

Introduction

Normal Seed Formation

In angiosperms, seeds are normally formed after meiosis and fertilization. The embryo develops from the zygote formed by fusion of male and female gametes.

However, in some plants, seeds or embryos may develop without normal sexual reproduction. Two important phenomena related to such abnormal development are apomixis and polyembryony.

Basic Meaning

Apomixis refers to seed or embryo formation without fertilization.

Polyembryony refers to the formation of more than one embryo in a single seed.

Both are important in embryology, plant breeding and agriculture.

Exam focus: Apomixis explains asexual seed formation, while polyembryony explains the occurrence of multiple embryos in one seed. Both show that embryo development in angiosperms is not always dependent only on normal fertilization.

Apomixis: Definition and Types

Apomixis is an important phenomenon in which a seed or embryo is formed without fertilization and without normal sexual reproduction. It is especially significant because it can preserve the genetic constitution of the parent plant.

Definition

Apomixis

Apomixis is the formation of seed or embryo without fertilization and without normal sexual reproduction.

In apomixis, the embryo may develop from an unfertilized egg, from other cells of the embryo sac, or from nucellar or integumentary cells.

In simple words, apomixis is a type of asexual reproduction through seeds.

The plants produced by apomixis are usually genetically similar to the mother plant because there is no fusion of male and female gametes.

Types of Apomixis

Apomixis is mainly classified into the following types:

TypeBasic ideaImportant point
Recurrent apomixisEmbryo sac develops from a diploid cell without meiosis.Maintains chromosome number and produces plants genetically similar to the parent.
Non-recurrent apomixisNormal meiosis forms a haploid embryo sac, but the egg develops without fertilization.Produces haploid, usually weak or sterile plants.
Adventive embryonyEmbryos arise directly from somatic cells outside the embryo sac.Commonly involves nucellus or integuments.
Vegetative apomixisVegetative propagules are formed in place of flowers or seeds.New plants arise without fertilization.
Type 1

Recurrent Apomixis

In recurrent apomixis, the embryo sac develops from a diploid cell without meiosis. Therefore, the egg cell is also diploid. This diploid egg develops into an embryo without fertilization.

Since meiosis is absent or suppressed, the chromosome number of the mother plant is maintained in the offspring. Therefore, recurrent apomixis produces plants genetically similar to the parent.

Recurrent apomixis may occur in two ways: diplospory and apospory.

A. Diplospory

In diplospory, the embryo sac develops directly from the megaspore mother cell without normal meiosis.

The megaspore mother cell behaves abnormally and forms an unreduced embryo sac.

The egg cell remains diploid and develops into an embryo without fertilization.

Example: Taraxacum.

B. Apospory

In apospory, the embryo sac develops from a somatic cell of the nucellus instead of the megaspore mother cell.

The normal sexual embryo sac may degenerate, and an unreduced embryo sac develops from nucellar tissue.

The egg cell in such an embryo sac is diploid and develops parthenogenetically.

Example: Hieracium, some grasses.

Type 2

Non-recurrent Apomixis

In non-recurrent apomixis, normal meiosis takes place and a haploid embryo sac is formed. The egg cell is haploid, but it develops into an embryo without fertilization.

Since the embryo develops from a haploid egg, the resulting plant is also haploid and is usually weak or sterile. Therefore, this type is called non-recurrent because it does not normally continue for many generations.

This type is less important agriculturally than recurrent apomixis.

Type 3

Adventive Embryony

In adventive embryony, embryos develop directly from somatic cells outside the embryo sac, especially from the nucellus or integuments. These embryos are not formed from the egg cell.

The adventive embryos grow into the embryo sac and develop along with or instead of the sexual embryo.

This type is common in plants such as Citrus and mango.

In Citrus, nucellar cells often give rise to embryos. These nucellar embryos are genetically identical to the mother plant.

Type 4

Vegetative Apomixis

In vegetative apomixis, vegetative propagules are formed in place of flowers or seeds. These structures detach and give rise to new plants.

For example, in some plants, bulbils may be formed instead of flowers. These bulbils grow into new plants without fertilization.

Example: Some species of Allium and grasses.

Significance of Apomixis

Significance: Apomixis is important because it produces offspring without fertilization. It maintains the genetic constitution of the parent plant and produces uniform progeny. It is especially useful in preserving hybrid characters and desirable traits.

Asexual seed formation

Apomixis allows seed formation without normal sexual reproduction and fertilization.

Maternal similarity

Since there is no fusion of gametes, the progeny is generally genetically similar to the mother plant.

Breeding value

It helps in preserving useful hybrid characters and desirable agronomic traits.

Polyembryony: Definition and Types

Polyembryony is the occurrence of more than one embryo in a single seed. It may arise through different developmental routes and is especially common in Citrus, mango and some gymnosperms.

Definition

Polyembryony

Polyembryony is the occurrence of more than one embryo in a single seed.

The embryos may arise from the zygote, from cells of the embryo sac, from more than one embryo sac, or from nucellar and integumentary cells.

Polyembryony was first observed by Leeuwenhoek in orange seeds.

It is commonly found in Citrus, mango and some gymnosperms.

Types of Polyembryony

Polyembryony may be classified in different ways. The important types are:

TypeDescriptionImportant example / point
True polyembryonyMore than one embryo develops inside the same embryo sac.Embryos are associated with one embryo sac.
False polyembryonyMore than one embryo develops because more than one embryo sac is present in the ovule.Embryos arise from different embryo sacs in the same ovule.
Cleavage polyembryonyThe zygote or young proembryo divides into several units, each forming a separate embryo.Common in some gymnosperms; rare in angiosperms.
Adventive polyembryonyAdditional embryos arise from somatic cells of the ovule, especially nucellus or integuments.Common in Citrus and mango.
Polyembryony from embryo sac cellsEmbryos develop from cells of the embryo sac other than the egg cell.Synergids, antipodals and other embryo sac cells may contribute.
Type 1

True Polyembryony

In true polyembryony, more than one embryo develops inside the same embryo sac.

The embryos may arise from the zygote, synergids, antipodal cells or nucellar cells entering the same embryo sac.

This is true polyembryony because multiple embryos are associated with one embryo sac.

Type 2

False Polyembryony

In false polyembryony, more than one embryo develops in a seed because more than one embryo sac is present in the ovule. Each embryo sac may produce an embryo.

Thus, the multiple embryos do not arise from a single embryo sac but from different embryo sacs present in the same ovule.

Type 3

Cleavage Polyembryony

In cleavage polyembryony, the zygote or young proembryo divides into several units, and each unit develops into a separate embryo.

This type is common in some gymnosperms but may also occur rarely in angiosperms.

In this case, all embryos originate from the same zygote.

Type 4

Adventive Polyembryony

In adventive polyembryony, additional embryos arise from somatic cells of the ovule, especially the nucellus or integuments. These embryos develop along with the normal zygotic embryo.

This is common in Citrus and mango.

In Citrus, nucellar cells divide and form several embryos. Therefore, one seed may contain both a sexual embryo and nucellar embryos.

Type 5

Polyembryony from Embryo Sac Cells

Sometimes embryos may develop from cells of the embryo sac other than the egg cell.

These may include synergids, antipodal cells and other embryo sac cells.

Cell giving rise to embryoType
SynergidsSynergid embryo
Antipodal cellsAntipodal embryo
Egg cellNormal zygotic embryo
Other cells of embryo sacAdditional embryos
Important point: In such cases, more than one embryo may be formed within a single embryo sac.

Topic 6
06

Practical Applications

Applications

Practical Applications of Apomixis

1. Fixation of Hybrid Vigour

Apomixis can preserve hybrid vigour or heterosis for many generations. In normal sexual reproduction, hybrid characters segregate in the next generation. But in apomixis, seeds produce plants genetically identical to the mother plant, so hybrid characters remain fixed.

2. Production of Uniform Crops

Apomictic seeds produce genetically uniform plants. This is useful in agriculture because farmers can get uniform yield, growth habit and quality.

3. Saving Cost of Hybrid Seed Production

Hybrid seeds are expensive because they have to be produced again and again. If apomixis is introduced into hybrid crops, farmers can reuse seeds without losing hybrid characters.

4. Maintenance of Desirable Characters

Apomixis helps preserve desirable characters such as high yield, disease resistance, stress tolerance and fruit quality.

5. Useful in Plant Breeding

Apomixis is valuable in plant breeding because it allows rapid multiplication of superior genotypes without genetic segregation.

Applications

Practical Applications of Polyembryony

1. Production of Nucellar Seedlings

In Citrus and mango, nucellar embryos produce seedlings genetically identical to the mother plant. These are useful for producing uniform rootstocks.

2. Disease-free Plants

Nucellar embryos are often free from certain pathogens. Therefore, nucellar seedlings are used for producing healthy planting material, especially in Citrus.

3. Clonal Propagation through Seeds

Polyembryony allows clonal multiplication through seeds. This is useful because seed propagation is easier than vegetative propagation in some plants.

4. Plant Breeding and Selection

Polyembryony is useful in plant breeding because it produces multiple seedlings from a single seed. Breeders can select vigorous and desirable seedlings.

5. Horticultural Importance

In Citrus, polyembryony is highly valuable for raising uniform and vigorous rootstocks. It helps maintain desirable maternal characters and improves orchard uniformity.

Topic 7
07

Significance and Conclusion

Significance

Importance in Reproduction, Agriculture and Breeding

Apomixis and polyembryony are important reproductive phenomena. Apomixis avoids fertilization and produces maternal clones through seeds. Polyembryony increases the number of embryos in a seed and may produce both sexual and asexual embryos.

Both processes are useful in crop improvement, horticulture and plant breeding.

They also show that embryo development in angiosperms is not always dependent only on normal fertilization.

Conclusion

Exam-ready Closing Paragraph

Apomixis is the formation of embryo or seed without fertilization, while polyembryony is the formation of more than one embryo in a single seed. Apomixis may be recurrent, non-recurrent, adventive or vegetative. Polyembryony may be true, false, cleavage, adventive or due to embryo sac cells. Both phenomena are important in plant reproduction and have practical applications in agriculture and horticulture.

Apomixis is useful for fixing hybrid vigour and maintaining desirable characters, while polyembryony is useful for producing nucellar seedlings, disease-free plants and uniform rootstocks, especially in Citrus and mango.
