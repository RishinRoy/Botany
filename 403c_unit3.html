Endosperm: Introduction and Origin

The endosperm is a nutritive tissue formed inside the seed of angiosperms after fertilization. It is one of the most important products of double fertilization because it nourishes the developing embryo and, in many seeds, stores reserve food for germination.

Introduction

The endosperm develops from the primary endosperm nucleus, which is formed during double fertilization. In most angiosperms, one male gamete fuses with the two polar nuclei or with the secondary nucleus to form a triploid primary endosperm nucleus.

This fusion of one male gamete with the polar nuclei is called triple fusion. After its formation, the primary endosperm nucleus divides repeatedly and gives rise to the endosperm tissue.

The main function of endosperm is to nourish the developing embryo. In many seeds, it also stores food and provides nutrition during seed germination.

Origin of Endosperm

Endosperm is formed as a result of triple fusion inside the embryo sac. The fusion may be represented as:

Male gamete (n) + two polar nuclei (n + n) = Primary endosperm nucleus (3n)

The primary endosperm nucleus is usually triploid. It divides mitotically and forms the endosperm tissue. Since endosperm develops only after fertilization, food material is not wasted before fertilization. This is one of the important advantages of double fertilization in angiosperms.

Types of Endosperm

On the basis of development, endosperm is mainly of three types: nuclear endosperm, cellular endosperm and helobial endosperm. The main difference among them is the time and pattern of cell wall formation after division of the primary endosperm nucleus.

Type 1

Nuclear Endosperm

Meaning

The nuclear endosperm is the most common type of endosperm in angiosperms. In this type, the primary endosperm nucleus undergoes repeated free nuclear divisions without immediate wall formation.

As a result, many free nuclei are formed inside the embryo sac. These nuclei remain embedded in the cytoplasm. Cell wall formation occurs later, either partly or completely.

Development

At first, the primary endosperm nucleus divides into two nuclei. These nuclei divide repeatedly, producing many free nuclei. The embryo sac becomes filled with multinucleate cytoplasm.

Later, walls may be formed around the nuclei, producing cellular endosperm tissue. In some plants, a part of the endosperm may remain free nuclear for some time.

Examples Nuclear endosperm is found in many common plants such as coconut, maize, wheat, rice and cotton. In coconut, coconut water represents the free nuclear stage of endosperm, while the white kernel represents the cellular endosperm.

Type 2

Cellular Endosperm

Meaning

In cellular endosperm, every division of the primary endosperm nucleus is followed by wall formation. Therefore, the endosperm becomes cellular from the beginning.

There is no free nuclear stage in this type. After the first division of the primary endosperm nucleus, a wall is formed between the two daughter nuclei. Further divisions are also followed by cell wall formation.

Development

The primary endosperm nucleus divides into two nuclei, and a wall is formed between them. These cells divide further, and each division is accompanied by wall formation.

Thus, a cellular endosperm tissue is formed directly from the beginning.

Examples Cellular endosperm is found in plants such as Datura, Petunia and Balsam.

Type 3

Helobial Endosperm

Meaning

The helobial endosperm is intermediate between nuclear and cellular types. It is commonly found in some monocots.

In this type, the first division of the primary endosperm nucleus is followed by formation of a transverse wall. This divides the embryo sac into two chambers: a large micropylar chamber and a small chalazal chamber.

Development

The primary endosperm nucleus first divides into two nuclei. A wall is formed between them, producing a large micropylar cell and a small chalazal cell.

The micropylar cell gives rise to most of the endosperm by free nuclear divisions. The chalazal cell may divide less actively, may form a few nuclei, may remain small, or may function as a haustorial region.

Examples Helobial endosperm is found in plants such as Eremurus, Najas and some members of Helobiales.

Other Forms of Endosperm

Apart from developmental types, endosperm may also be described on the basis of its persistence in the mature seed and its structure.

Albuminous or Endospermic Seeds

In some seeds, the endosperm persists even in the mature seed. Such seeds are called albuminous or endospermic seeds.

Examples: castor, maize, wheat, rice and coconut.

In these seeds, the embryo uses the stored food of endosperm during germination.

Exalbuminous or Non-endospermic Seeds

In some seeds, the endosperm is completely consumed by the developing embryo before seed maturity. Such seeds are called exalbuminous or non-endospermic seeds.

Examples: pea, gram and bean.

In these seeds, food is stored mainly in the cotyledons.

Ruminate Endosperm

In some seeds, the endosperm becomes irregularly folded due to the ingrowth of seed coat tissues. Such endosperm is called ruminate endosperm.

Examples: areca nut, Annona and Myristica.

Structure and Functions of Endosperm

Structure

Structure of Endosperm

The structure of endosperm varies in different plants, but it is generally a nutritive tissue surrounding or lying near the embryo. In many seeds, the endosperm is made up of living parenchymatous cells that store reserve food materials such as starch, proteins and oils.

In cereals, the endosperm is well developed and differentiated into two main regions: the aleurone layer and the starchy endosperm.

Aleurone Layer

The aleurone layer is the outer protein-rich layer of the endosperm, especially in cereals such as wheat, rice and maize. It consists of living cells rich in proteins and enzymes.

During germination, the aleurone layer secretes enzymes such as amylase. These enzymes digest stored starch and convert it into simple sugars for the growing embryo.

Starchy Endosperm

The starchy endosperm forms the major part of cereal grains. It consists of cells filled with starch grains and is usually non-living at maturity.

This region provides carbohydrates to the embryo during germination. In cereals, it forms the main edible portion of the grain.

Endosperm Haustoria

In some plants, parts of the endosperm become modified into haustoria. These structures help in absorbing nutrients from the surrounding nucellus or integuments and transfer them to the developing embryo and endosperm.

Endosperm haustoria are commonly seen in some plants where the developing embryo requires active nutrition from surrounding tissues.

Functions

Functions of Endosperm

Endosperm performs several important functions in angiosperms. It acts as a nutritive tissue, food-storage tissue and economically important food source.

1. Nutrition of Developing Embryo

The primary function of endosperm is to provide food to the developing embryo. It supplies carbohydrates, proteins, fats, minerals and growth substances.

2. Food Storage

In many seeds, the endosperm stores reserve food materials. These may include starch, oils and proteins. For example, cereals store starch in the endosperm, while castor stores oils.

3. Nourishment During Germination

During seed germination, the stored food in the endosperm is broken down and supplied to the growing embryo. This helps the seedling grow until it becomes photosynthetic.

4. Enzyme Production

In cereal grains, the aleurone layer produces enzymes during germination. These enzymes convert stored starch into soluble sugars, which are used by the embryo.

5. Economical Use

Endosperm is economically very important. The edible part of cereals such as rice, wheat and maize is mainly endosperm. Coconut water and coconut kernel are also endospermic tissues. Many food products are obtained from endosperm-rich seeds.

6. Efficient Use of Food Material

Endosperm develops only after fertilization. Therefore, nutritive tissue is formed only when embryo development is possible. This prevents wastage of food material and is an important advantage of angiosperms.

Significance

Endosperm is a characteristic nutritive tissue of angiosperms. It supports embryo development and increases seed survival.

In albuminous seeds, endosperm remains stored in the mature seed and nourishes the embryo during germination. In exalbuminous seeds, it is consumed by the embryo and food is stored in cotyledons.

The formation of endosperm after fertilization shows the efficiency of angiosperm reproduction because food material is produced only after successful fertilization.

Conclusion

Endosperm is a nutritive tissue formed from the primary endosperm nucleus after triple fusion. It is usually triploid and provides nourishment to the developing embryo.

Based on development, endosperm is of three main types: nuclear, cellular and helobial. Nuclear endosperm has free nuclear divisions, cellular endosperm has wall formation from the beginning, and helobial endosperm is intermediate.

Structurally, endosperm may be starchy, protein-rich or oily. In cereals, it is differentiated into aleurone layer and starchy endosperm. Its main functions are nourishment of embryo, food storage, enzyme production during germination and economic importance as food material. Thus, endosperm is one of the most important tissues in angiosperm seeds.

Development of Dicot Embryo

In angiosperms, the embryo develops from the zygote formed after syngamy. Syngamy is the fusion of one male gamete with the egg cell during double fertilization. The zygote is diploid and represents the first cell of the sporophytic generation.

The development of embryo from the zygote is called embryogeny. In dicots, the embryo usually develops two cotyledons. Development of the dicot embryo is best studied in Capsella bursa-pastoris. This type of embryogeny is called the Crucifer type or Onagrad type.

Dicot embryo

Stages in Development of Dicot Embryo

1. Zygote

After fertilization, the egg cell becomes the zygote. The zygote becomes elongated and divides transversely into two unequal cells: the terminal cell and the basal cell.

The terminal cell lies towards the chalazal end, while the basal cell lies towards the micropylar end.

2. Formation of Terminal and Basal Cells

The terminal cell gives rise mainly to the embryo proper. The basal cell gives rise mainly to the suspensor.

Terminal cellEmbryo proper
Basal cellSuspensor and part of root apex

3. Development of Suspensor

The basal cell divides transversely several times and forms a filamentous structure called the suspensor. The suspensor pushes the developing embryo into the endosperm so that it can receive nutrition.

The uppermost cell of the suspensor, lying near the embryo proper, is called the hypophysis. The hypophysis contributes to the formation of the root apex and root cap.

4. Development of Embryo Proper

The terminal cell first divides vertically into two cells. These two cells divide by another vertical wall at right angles to the first division, forming four cells. This is called the quadrant stage.

Each of the four cells then divides transversely to form eight cells. This is called the octant stage.

5. Globular Stage

The cells of the octant embryo divide further and form a rounded mass of cells called the globular embryo.

At this stage, the outer cells divide periclinally and form a distinct outer layer called the dermatogen or protoderm. This layer gives rise to the epidermis.

The inner cells differentiate into periblem, which forms the cortex, and plerome, which forms the vascular tissue. Thus, the basic tissue regions of the embryo begin to differentiate at the globular stage.

6. Heart-shaped Stage

As development continues, two lateral outgrowths appear at the upper region of the embryo. These outgrowths are the two cotyledons.

Due to the formation of two cotyledons, the embryo becomes heart-shaped. This is called the heart-shaped stage.

The region between the two cotyledons develops into the plumule, which later forms the shoot apex.

7. Torpedo Stage

The cotyledons and embryonal axis elongate further, and the embryo becomes elongated. This stage is called the torpedo stage.

At this stage, the main parts of the embryo become distinct: cotyledons, plumule, hypocotyl and radicle.

8. Mature Dicot Embryo

The mature dicot embryo has two cotyledons attached to an embryonal axis.

The part of the embryonal axis above the cotyledons is called the epicotyl, and it bears the plumule. The part below the cotyledons is called the hypocotyl, and it ends in the radicle.

The radicle later forms the primary root, while the plumule forms the shoot system.

Development of Monocot Embryo

Monocot embryo development is commonly studied in Sagittaria and grasses such as maize. The basic early stages are similar to dicots, but the mature embryo has only one cotyledon.

Monocot embryo

Stages in Development of Monocot Embryo

1. Zygote

The zygote is formed by fusion of the male gamete with the egg cell. It enlarges and divides transversely into two cells: the terminal cell and the basal cell.

The terminal cell usually forms most of the embryo proper, while the basal cell forms the suspensor.

2. Formation of Proembryo

The terminal cell divides repeatedly to form a mass of cells. This early multicellular stage is called the proembryo.

The basal cell divides to form the suspensor. The suspensor helps in pushing the developing embryo into the nutritive endosperm.

3. Differentiation of Embryo

As the embryo develops, it becomes differentiated into the embryonal axis and a single cotyledon.

In monocots, the single cotyledon becomes highly specialized. In grasses, it is called the scutellum. The scutellum lies on one side of the embryonal axis and helps in absorption of food from the endosperm during germination.

4. Formation of Plumule and Radicle

The embryonal axis differentiates into two main regions: the plumule and the radicle.

The plumule forms the future shoot system. In grasses, it is protected by a sheath called the coleoptile.

The radicle forms the future root system. In grasses, it is protected by a sheath called the coleorhiza.

Mature stage

Mature Monocot Embryo

A mature monocot embryo has only one cotyledon. In grasses, the embryo is situated on one side of the endosperm. The scutellum secretes enzymes and absorbs digested food from the endosperm during germination.

Significance of Embryo Development

Embryo development is important because it gives rise to the future plant. The zygote develops into a young sporophyte with root and shoot apices.

The radicle forms the primary root, while the plumule forms the shoot system. Cotyledons help in storage or absorption of food. In monocots, the scutellum helps in absorbing food from the endosperm.

Thus, embryogeny establishes the basic body plan of the plant.

Conclusion

Embryo development in angiosperms begins with the zygote. In dicots such as Capsella, the zygote divides into terminal and basal cells. The terminal cell forms the embryo proper, while the basal cell forms the suspensor.

The embryo passes through quadrant, octant, globular, heart-shaped and torpedo stages, finally forming a mature embryo with two cotyledons, plumule, hypocotyl and radicle.

In monocots, embryo development is similar at first, but finally forms only one cotyledon. In grasses, this cotyledon is called the scutellum, and the plumule and radicle are protected by coleoptile and coleorhiza respectively. Thus, dicot and monocot embryos differ mainly in the number of cotyledons and in the special protective structures present in monocots.
