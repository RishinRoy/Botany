Topic 1 · 10-mark answer
01

Fossils: Definition, Types, Nomenclature, Fossilization and Geological Time Scale

Fossils are very important in botany because they provide direct evidence about plants that lived in the past. The study of plant fossils is called palaeobotany. Fossils help us understand the origin, evolution, structure, distribution and extinction of ancient plants. They are especially useful in studying extinct groups such as seed ferns, ancient pteridophytes and primitive gymnosperms.

1. Definition of Fossils

The term fossil is derived from the Latin word fossilis, meaning “dug out.” In botany, a fossil may be defined as the preserved remains, impressions, traces or evidence of ancient plants that lived in past geological ages and are found preserved in rocks, sediments, coal, amber or other natural deposits.

Plant fossils may include leaves, stems, roots, seeds, spores, pollen grains, wood, fruits or even impressions of plant parts. They may preserve only the external form of the plant or, in some cases, even the internal anatomy.

2 · Types of fossils
02

Types of Plant Fossils

Plant fossils are of different types depending on how the plant material is preserved.

A. Compression fossils

In compression fossils, the plant part is flattened due to pressure of sediments. A thin carbonaceous film of the original plant material may remain. Leaves are commonly preserved as compressions. They show the external form of the plant but usually do not preserve detailed internal anatomy.

Example: Fossil leaves of Glossopteris.

B. Impression fossils

In impression fossils, the plant part leaves only an imprint on the surrounding rock or sediment. The original plant material is absent. Only the shape, outline or surface details are preserved. Impressions are common in leaves, bark and stems.

Example: Leaf impressions of ferns.

C. Petrified fossils

In petrified fossils, the plant tissues are replaced or infiltrated by minerals such as silica, calcium carbonate or iron compounds. This type of fossil is very important because it may preserve the internal anatomy of the plant and is therefore highly useful in studying extinct plants.

Example: Petrified wood.

D. Casts and moulds

A mould is formed when a plant part buried in sediment decays and leaves a hollow cavity. A cast is formed when this cavity is later filled with mineral matter or sediment. Casts and moulds preserve the external form of the plant part.

Example: Stem casts of ancient plants.

E. Coal balls

Coal balls are rounded masses of petrified plant material found in coal deposits. They are formed by mineral infiltration, commonly by calcium carbonate and other minerals. Coal balls are very valuable in palaeobotany because both morphology and anatomy may be preserved.

Example: Coal balls containing fossils of ancient pteridophytes.

F. Amber fossils

Amber fossils are formed when plant parts or small organisms become trapped in resin that later hardens into amber. In plants, pollen grains, spores, small flowers or plant fragments may be preserved in amber, often showing fine structural details.

G. Actual remains

Sometimes plant parts are preserved almost unchanged under special conditions such as extreme cold, dryness or lack of oxygen. These are called actual remains. They are rare in plants but very valuable.

Example: Preserved seeds, wood or pollen grains.

3 · Nomenclature
03

Nomenclature of Plant Fossils

The nomenclature of plant fossils is different from that of living plants because fossil specimens are often incomplete. A fossil plant may be represented only by a leaf, stem, root, seed or reproductive structure. Since the whole plant is rarely preserved, separate names are often given to different fossil parts.

A. Form genus

A form genus is a name given to a fossil plant part whose complete plant is not known. For example, a fossil leaf, stem or root may be named separately even if they belonged to the same original plant.

Example: Glossopteris is commonly used for fossil leaves, while other organs of the same plant may have different names.

B. Organ genus

An organ genus is used for a particular organ of a fossil plant, such as a leaf, stem, root, pollen organ or seed. This helps palaeobotanists describe incomplete fossil material scientifically.

C. Reconstruction of fossil plants

When different fossil parts are found associated with each other and their connection is proved, they may be reconstructed as parts of one plant. Until then, separate fossil names are used for different organs.

D. Importance of fossil nomenclature

Fossil nomenclature helps in accurate identification, comparison and classification of fossil plants. It also avoids confusion because a fossil plant is rarely found as a complete organism.

4 · Fossilization
04

Fossilization

Fossilization is the process by which plant remains or traces are preserved in the earth’s crust for long periods of geological time. Fossilization usually occurs when plant parts are quickly buried under sediments such as mud, sand or volcanic ash. Burial protects the plant material from complete decay.

Conditions necessary for fossilization Good fossilization usually requires rapid burial, absence of oxygen, presence of mineral-rich water, pressure from sediments and resistance of plant parts to decay. Hard parts such as wood, seeds, spores and pollen are more easily preserved than delicate parts.
Main processes of fossilization

A. Compression

The plant part is buried under sediments and becomes flattened due to pressure. Some carbonaceous matter may remain as a thin film.

B. Impression

The plant part leaves an imprint on soft sediment. Later the sediment hardens into rock, preserving the impression.

C. Petrifaction

Minerals enter the plant tissues and replace or fill the cells. This may preserve even cellular details.

D. Carbonization

Volatile substances are lost from the plant body, and a thin carbon film remains. This is common in leaf fossils.

E. Cast and mould formation

The plant part decays after burial, leaving a cavity called a mould. If the cavity is filled by minerals or sediments, a cast is formed.

F. Amber preservation

Plant parts get trapped in resin, which later hardens into amber.

5 · Geological time scale
05

Geological Time Scale

The geological time scale is a chronological arrangement of the history of the earth. It divides earth history into eons, eras, periods and epochs. It is important in palaeobotany because fossils are studied according to the geological period in which they occur. The International Commission on Stratigraphy maintains the official International Chronostratigraphic Chart, which provides the standard global time divisions used for geological history.

Topic 1 · Conclusion
06

Conclusion

Fossils are preserved remains, impressions or traces of ancient plants found in rocks and sediments. They are studied under palaeobotany and are important for understanding plant evolution, extinct plant groups and past environments. Plant fossils may occur as compressions, impressions, petrifactions, casts, moulds, coal balls, amber fossils or actual remains. Because fossil plants are often incomplete, fossil nomenclature uses form genera and organ genera. Fossilization occurs through processes such as compression, impression, petrifaction, carbonization and amber preservation. The geological time scale provides the time framework for arranging fossils and understanding the history of plant evolution.

General Account of Psilophytales, Lepidodendrales, Sphenophyllales and Bennettitales

With special reference to Rhynia, Lepidodendron, Sphenophyllum and Williamsonia.

Fossil plants are very important in botany because they show the evolutionary history of plant groups. Some fossil plants represent early vascular plants, while others represent extinct pteridophytes or gymnosperms. Psilophytales, Lepidodendrales and Sphenophyllales are extinct pteridophytic groups, while Bennettitales is an extinct gymnosperm group. Their study helps us understand the origin of vascular plants, evolution of leaves, stele, spores, cones and seed habit.

1 · Psilophytales
08

Psilophytales with Special Reference to Rhynia

Occurrence and geological age

Psilophytales includes some of the earliest and simplest vascular plants. Rhynia is a well-known fossil member of this group. It was discovered from the Rhynie chert of Scotland and belongs to the Early Devonian period. Rhynia gwynne-vaughanii is regarded as one of the earliest vascular land plants.

Morphology

The plant body of Rhynia was very simple. It had a creeping underground rhizome-like axis and upright aerial branches. True roots and true leaves were absent. Rhizoids were present for absorption. The aerial axes were cylindrical, leafless, green and dichotomously branched.

Anatomy

Internally, the stem showed an epidermis, cortex and central vascular cylinder. The stele was protostelic, with xylem in the centre and phloem surrounding it. The xylem contained tracheids, proving that Rhynia was a true vascular plant, although primitive.

Reproduction

Rhynia reproduced by spores. The sporangia were borne terminally at the tips of aerial branches. Spores were produced inside the sporangia and were probably dispersed by wind. It was homosporous.

Evolutionary significance Rhynia is important because it represents a very primitive stage in the evolution of vascular plants. Its simple body, absence of roots and leaves, dichotomous branching and protostele make it a key fossil in the study of early land plant evolution.
2 · Lepidodendrales
09

Lepidodendrales with Special Reference to Lepidodendron

Occurrence and geological age

Lepidodendrales was an extinct group of arborescent lycopods. Lepidodendron is the best-known member of this order. It was abundant during the Carboniferous period, when large swamp forests were formed. These plants contributed greatly to coal formation.

Morphology

Lepidodendron was a tall tree-like plant. The stem was erect, massive and dichotomously branched near the top. The stem surface had characteristic diamond-shaped leaf scars, giving it the name “scale tree.” Leaves were small, simple, linear and microphyllous. Underground rooting organs were known as Stigmaria.

Anatomy

The stem showed a protostelic or siphonostelic vascular system depending on age and species. The central vascular cylinder was relatively small compared with the huge cortex and bark. Secondary growth was present, but much of the support came from thick outer cortex and periderm.

Reproduction

Reproductive structures were organized into cones or strobili bearing sporophylls with sporangia. Many members of Lepidodendrales were heterosporous, producing microspores and megaspores. The related fossil form Lepidocarpon showed seed-like features and is important in understanding the origin of seed habit.

Evolutionary significance Lepidodendron shows that lycopods were once large forest-forming trees, unlike their small modern representatives. It also shows the importance of heterospory in the evolution of seed habit, and Carboniferous forests of Lepidodendron played a major role in coal formation.
3 · Sphenophyllales
10

Sphenophyllales with Special Reference to Sphenophyllum

Occurrence and geological age

Sphenophyllales was an extinct group related to the horsetail line. Sphenophyllum was common in the late Palaeozoic, especially from the Devonian to Permian periods. It is considered a close relative of the sphenopsids, the group that includes Equisetum.

Morphology

Sphenophyllum was a small, delicate, herbaceous or scrambling plant. The stem was slender, jointed and divided into nodes and internodes. Leaves were arranged in whorls at the nodes and were usually wedge-shaped, which explains the name “wedge leaf.”

Anatomy

The stem had a simple protostele. The primary xylem was usually triangular in outline, a characteristic feature of Sphenophyllum. Secondary growth was present in some species. Although the stem was jointed like Equisetum, the vascular cylinder remained more primitive.

Reproduction

The reproductive organs were borne in compact cones or strobili. These strobili consisted of whorls of sporophylls bearing sporangia. Most species were homosporous, although some advanced forms showed tendencies towards heterospory.

Evolutionary significance Sphenophyllum is important because it connects primitive pteridophytes with the horsetail group. It combines a protostele like early vascular plants with jointed stems and whorled leaves like sphenopsids, helping us understand the evolution of Equisetum-like plants.
4 · Bennettitales
11

Bennettitales with Special Reference to Williamsonia

Occurrence and geological age

Bennettitales is an extinct order of gymnosperms. It was especially common during the Mesozoic era, particularly the Jurassic and Cretaceous periods. Williamsonia is an important member. Bennettitalean plants often resembled cycads in vegetative structure but had more complex reproductive organs.

Morphology

Williamsonia was a cycad-like plant with a stout or branched stem. The leaves were large, pinnately compound and arranged spirally. The general appearance was similar to cycads, but the plant body was gymnospermous and seed-bearing.

Anatomy

The stem anatomy showed a eustele-like vascular organization. Secondary growth was present. The wood was gymnospermous, mainly with tracheids. The leaves had a thick cuticle and xerophytic features suitable for Mesozoic conditions.

Reproduction

The reproductive structures were large and flower-like and were usually borne at the ends of branches or in leaf axils. In Williamsonia, the reproductive structures were generally unisexual. The female structure had a receptacle bearing ovules surrounded by protective bracts, while the male structures had microsporophylls bearing synangia. They were flower-like, but not true angiosperm flowers.

Evolutionary significance Williamsonia shows advanced reproductive organization among fossil gymnosperms. Its flower-like reproductive structures created interest in the possible relationship between Bennettitales and angiosperms. However, Bennettitales were not true flowering plants because their ovules were not enclosed inside an ovary.

Conclusion Psilophytales, Lepidodendrales, Sphenophyllales and Bennettitales represent important fossil plant groups. Rhynia shows the earliest stage of vascular plant evolution with a rootless, leafless and protostelic body. Lepidodendron represents giant Carboniferous lycopods that contributed to coal formation. Sphenophyllum shows the extinct sphenopsid line related to horsetails. Williamsonia represents Bennettitales, an extinct gymnosperm group with flower-like reproductive structures. Thus, these fossils are highly significant in understanding the evolution of vascular plants, pteridophytes and gymnosperms.
