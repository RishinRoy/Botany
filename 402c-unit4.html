Topic 1 · Apex organisation
01

Theories of Structural Organisation of Root Apex and Shoot Apex

The growing tips of roots and shoots contain actively dividing meristematic tissue. These regions are called apical meristems. The root apex gives rise to primary tissues of the root, while the shoot apex gives rise to the stem, leaves, branches and floral organs. Many botanists proposed theories to explain the structural organisation and activity of these apical meristems. Important theories include apical cell theory, histogen theory, tunica-corpus theory, mantle-core concept and quiescent centre concept.

Shoot apex
02

Theories of Structural Organisation of Shoot Apex

Added from pages 2–5: Basic meaning of shoot apex and stem Shoot apex is the apical part or tip of the stem. It contains multipotent stem cells and an active apical meristem. It produces leaf primordia and gives rise to all aerial parts of the plant such as leaves, branches, flowers and fruits. Since it possesses apical meristem, it continues to grow and is responsible for primary growth of the shoot.

Stem, nodes, internodes and buds

The stem is the aerial part of the plant that bears leaves, branches, flowers and fruits. It is made up of nodes, where leaves are borne, and internodes, which are the portions present between two nodes.

The stem bears terminal buds and axillary buds. Axillary buds are present in the axils of leaves and develop into branches or flowers. These buds are formed from cells of the apical meristem that are left behind during stem elongation or leaf formation.

Apical meristem and primary growth

The apical meristem is present at the apical part of both root and shoot. It is a zone of active cell division and is responsible for growth in plants. It gives rise to primary tissues and brings about increase in length or height of the plant.

The shoot apex contains the apical meristem below which other tissues are differentiated. Therefore, the shoot apex forms the main region from which the young primary body of the shoot develops.

Structure and Organisation of Shoot Apex

Main structural features

The shoot apex is the tip of the stem containing meristematic cells and the region of growth. It develops into lateral branches, leaves and flowers. Its size, shape, organisation and pattern of tissue differentiation vary in different plant groups such as pteridophytes, gymnosperms and angiosperms.

It is present just above the youngest leaf primordium. It grows continuously and produces leaf primordia one after another.

Determinate and indeterminate growth

In the vegetative condition, the shoot apex generally shows continuous or indeterminate growth. However, when the shoot apex forms an inflorescence or flower, the growth becomes determinate.

The growth also becomes determinate or limited when the stem is modified into special structures such as tendrils or thorns.

Role in flower embryogenesis

The shoot apical meristem is the site of flower embryogenesis. Primordia of floral organs such as leaves, sepals, petals, stamens and ovaries start developing one by one.

The time interval between the initiation of successive primordia is known as a plastochron.

Four types of cells in shoot apical meristem

The shoot apical meristem consists of four functional cell types:

Stem cells: self-renewing cells that maintain the meristem.

Daughter stem cells: cells derived from stem cells that continue division and differentiation.

Founder cells: cells that initiate organ development such as leaves and floral parts.

Organising centre: a central regulatory region that helps maintain the activity and arrangement of the shoot apical meristem.

Primary meristems produced by apical meristem Apical meristems give rise to three primary meristems: protoderm, which differentiates into the epidermis; procambium, which gives rise to primary vascular tissues such as xylem and phloem; and ground meristem, which develops into cortex, pith, medulla and pericycle. Thus, the apical meristem is responsible for primary growth, increase in length and organ formation in plants.

1. Apical Cell Theory

The apical cell theory was proposed by Nägeli. According to this theory, the entire shoot body originates from a single apical cell present at the tip of the shoot apex. This apical cell divides repeatedly and gives rise to all tissues of the plant body.

This theory is mainly applicable to lower plants such as many algae, bryophytes and some pteridophytes. In gymnosperms and angiosperms, the shoot apex is not controlled by a single apical cell. Instead, it consists of a group of meristematic cells. Therefore, this theory is not suitable for explaining the shoot apex of higher plants.

2. Histogen Theory

The histogen theory was proposed by Hanstein. According to this theory, the shoot apex consists of three distinct meristematic zones called histogens. Each histogen gives rise to a particular tissue system.

HistogenTissue formed
DermatogenEpidermis
PeriblemCortex
PleromeStele or vascular cylinder

According to this theory, the outermost layer dermatogen gives rise to the epidermis. The middle region, periblem, forms the cortex. The central region, plerome, forms the vascular tissue and pith.

3. Tunica-Corpus Theory

The tunica-corpus theory was proposed by Schmidt. It is one of the most accepted theories for the organization of the shoot apex in angiosperms. According to this theory, the shoot apex is divided into two main regions: tunica and corpus.

RegionMain featureFunction
TunicaOuter one or more layers of cellsForms epidermis and sometimes outer tissues
CorpusInner mass of cellsForms cortex, vascular tissue and pith

The cells of tunica divide mainly by anticlinal divisions, maintaining the layered structure. The cells of corpus divide in different planes and add bulk to the shoot apex. This theory is important because it explains the layered organization of the shoot apex and the origin of different tissues in flowering plants.

4. Mantle-Core Concept

The mantle-core concept is similar to the tunica-corpus theory. According to this concept, the shoot apex has an outer mantle and an inner core. The mantle forms the outer covering layers of the apex, while the core forms the inner mass of tissues.

The mantle mainly contributes to surface growth, while the core contributes to the internal tissues of the stem. This concept is useful because it gives a simple explanation of shoot apex organization, but it is less precise than the tunica-corpus theory.

Root apex
03

Theories of Structural Organisation of Root Apex

1. Apical Cell Theory

According to the apical cell theory, the root apex develops from a single apical cell. This apical cell cuts off derivatives and gives rise to the tissues of the root.

This theory is applicable mainly to lower vascular plants such as some pteridophytes. In many ferns, the root apex has a single tetrahedral apical cell. However, in gymnosperms and angiosperms, the root apex usually has a group of initials instead of a single apical cell.

2. Histogen Theory

The histogen theory is more clearly applicable to the root apex than to the shoot apex. According to Hanstein’s histogen theory, the root apex consists of three histogens: dermatogen, periblem and plerome.

RegionTissue produced
DermatogenEpiblema or root epidermis
PeriblemCortex
PleromeVascular cylinder
CalyptrogenRoot cap

In many roots, an additional meristematic region called calyptrogen is also present. Calyptrogen gives rise to the root cap. This theory explains the origin of root tissues because the root apex often shows more definite tissue zones than the shoot apex.

3. Korper-Kappe Theory

The Korper-Kappe theory was proposed by Schüepp. It explains the organization of the root apex on the basis of planes of cell division.

RegionMeaningFunction
KorperBodyForms the main body of the root
KappeCapForms the root cap region

In the Korper region, cells divide in such a way that a T-shaped pattern is formed with the cross wall at the top. In the Kappe region, the T-shaped division pattern is inverted. This difference helps distinguish the root body from the root cap.

4. Quiescent Centre Concept

The quiescent centre concept was proposed by Clowes. According to this concept, the root apex contains a small group of cells near the centre of the meristem that divide very slowly or remain almost inactive. This region is called the quiescent centre.

The cells around the quiescent centre are more active and give rise to different tissues of the root. The quiescent centre acts as a reserve meristem. If the active meristematic cells are damaged, the cells of the quiescent centre can become active and help in regeneration.

Significance of these theories These theories explain how the primary plant body is formed from apical meristems. The apical cell theory explains meristem organization in lower plants. The histogen theory explains the origin of tissue systems. The tunica-corpus theory explains the layered organization of the shoot apex in angiosperms. The Korper-Kappe theory explains root apex organization by patterns of cell division. The quiescent centre concept explains the presence of slowly dividing reserve cells in the root apex.
Conclusion The structural organization of root and shoot apices has been explained by several theories. The shoot apex is best explained by the tunica-corpus theory, while root apex organization is explained by histogen theory, Korper-Kappe theory and quiescent centre concept. Together, these theories explain how root and shoot apical meristems produce the primary body of plants.
Topic 2 · Plant tissue
05

Different Types of Tissues, Tissue Systems and Mechanical Tissue

A tissue is a group of cells having a common origin and usually performing a common function. In higher plants, the plant body is made up of different types of tissues organized into tissue systems. These tissues help in growth, protection, photosynthesis, storage, conduction of water and food, and mechanical support. On the basis of their power of division, plant tissues are broadly divided into meristematic tissues and permanent tissues.

1. Different Types of Plant Tissues

A. Meristematic Tissue

Meristematic tissue is made up of actively dividing cells. These cells are responsible for the growth of the plant body. The cells are usually small, thin-walled, compactly arranged and have dense cytoplasm, prominent nucleus and little or no vacuole.

Apical meristem

It is present at the tips of roots and shoots. It brings about increase in length and is responsible for primary growth.

Intercalary meristem

It is present at the base of leaves or internodes, especially in grasses. It helps in elongation of internodes and regeneration of cut parts.

Lateral meristem

It is present along the sides of roots and stems. It brings about increase in thickness and is responsible for secondary growth. Examples are vascular cambium and cork cambium.

B. Permanent Tissue

Permanent tissues are formed from meristematic tissues. Their cells have lost the power of active division and are specialized for particular functions. Permanent tissues are mainly of two types: simple permanent tissue and complex permanent tissue.

Simple permanent tissues
06

Simple Permanent Tissues

Simple permanent tissues are made up of only one type of cell. They include parenchyma, collenchyma and sclerenchyma.

A. Parenchyma

Parenchyma is a living simple tissue made up of thin-walled cells. The cells are usually oval, spherical or polygonal and have intercellular spaces. The cell wall is made of cellulose.

Parenchyma performs storage of food, photosynthesis, secretion, wound healing and regeneration. When parenchyma contains chloroplasts, it is called chlorenchyma. When it contains large air spaces, it is called aerenchyma, common in aquatic plants.

B. Collenchyma

Collenchyma is a living mechanical tissue. Its cells are elongated and have unevenly thickened walls, especially at the corners. The thickening is mainly due to cellulose and pectin.

It is commonly found below the epidermis of young dicot stems, petioles and leaf margins. It provides mechanical support and flexibility to growing organs, allowing young parts to bend without breaking.

C. Sclerenchyma

Sclerenchyma is a dead mechanical tissue made up of thick-walled cells. The walls are heavily lignified. Mature sclerenchyma cells usually lack protoplasm.

Sclerenchyma provides hardness, rigidity and mechanical strength. It is of two main types: fibres and sclereids. Fibres are long, narrow and pointed cells, while sclereids are short, thick-walled cells found in hard parts such as seed coats, nutshells and gritty pulp of pear.

Complex permanent tissues
07

Complex Permanent Tissues: Xylem and Phloem

Complex tissues are made up of more than one type of cell working together for a common function. The two main complex tissues are xylem and phloem.

A. Xylem

Xylem is a complex tissue responsible mainly for the conduction of water and minerals from roots to other parts of the plant. It also provides mechanical support.

Tracheids: Dead, elongated cells with lignified walls. They conduct water and provide support.

Vessels: Dead, tube-like structures formed by vessel elements. They conduct water efficiently and are common in angiosperms.

Xylem fibres: Dead, thick-walled cells that provide mechanical strength.

Xylem parenchyma: Living cells that help in storage and lateral conduction.

B. Phloem

Phloem is a complex tissue responsible for the conduction of prepared food from leaves to other parts of the plant.

Sieve tubes: Living conducting elements that transport food in angiosperms.

Companion cells: Living cells associated with sieve tubes. They control and support the activity of sieve tubes.

Phloem parenchyma: Living cells that store food and help in lateral transport.

Phloem fibres: Thick-walled cells that provide mechanical support.

Tissue systems
08

Tissue Systems

According to Sachs, the tissues of a plant body are organized into three tissue systems: epidermal tissue system, ground tissue system and vascular tissue system.

A. Epidermal Tissue System

The epidermal tissue system forms the outer covering of the plant body. It includes the epidermis, cuticle, stomata, trichomes and root hairs.

The epidermis is usually single-layered and protects the inner tissues. The cuticle reduces water loss from aerial parts. Stomata regulate gaseous exchange and transpiration. Root hairs help in absorption of water and minerals from soil. Trichomes protect the plant from excessive heat, water loss and herbivores.

B. Ground Tissue System

The ground tissue system includes all tissues except the epidermal and vascular tissues. It mainly consists of parenchyma, collenchyma and sclerenchyma.

In roots and stems, it forms the cortex, endodermis, pericycle, pith and medullary rays. In leaves, it forms the mesophyll tissue. The ground tissue system performs photosynthesis, storage, secretion and mechanical support.

C. Vascular Tissue System

The vascular tissue system consists of xylem and phloem. These tissues are arranged in vascular bundles. Xylem conducts water and minerals, while phloem conducts prepared food.

The vascular tissue system also provides mechanical strength to the plant body. In roots, vascular tissues are usually arranged radially. In stems and leaves, they are arranged in vascular bundles.

Mechanical tissue
09

Mechanical Tissue

Mechanical tissue provides strength, rigidity and support to the plant body. It helps plants withstand bending, pulling, wind pressure and the weight of branches, leaves, flowers and fruits. The main mechanical tissues are collenchyma, sclerenchyma and xylem fibres.

A. Collenchyma as mechanical tissue

Collenchyma provides mechanical support to young growing parts. It is living and flexible, so it allows growth while giving strength. It is commonly present below the epidermis of young dicot stems and petioles. It prevents tearing of soft plant parts.

B. Sclerenchyma as mechanical tissue

Sclerenchyma provides strong mechanical support to mature parts of plants. Its cells are dead, thick-walled and lignified. Fibres give tensile strength, while sclereids provide hardness. It is found in stems, seed coats, nutshells and vascular bundles.

C. Xylem as mechanical tissue

Besides conduction, xylem also provides mechanical strength due to lignified tracheids, vessels and xylem fibres. Woody plants are strong mainly because of the presence of secondary xylem.

Conclusion Plant tissues are broadly divided into meristematic and permanent tissues. Meristematic tissues bring about growth, while permanent tissues perform specialized functions. Simple tissues include parenchyma, collenchyma and sclerenchyma, while complex tissues include xylem and phloem. These tissues are organized into epidermal, ground and vascular tissue systems. Mechanical tissues such as collenchyma, sclerenchyma and xylem fibres provide strength and support to the plant body. Thus, tissues and tissue systems together form the structural and functional framework of higher plants.
