Pollination: Types, Mechanism and Adaptations

With special reference to Anemophily, Hydrophily and Entomophily

Introduction

Pollination is the transfer of pollen grains from the anther to the stigma of a flower. It is an essential step in sexual reproduction of angiosperms because it brings the male gametophyte near the female reproductive organ. After successful pollination, the pollen grain germinates on the stigma and produces a pollen tube, which carries the male gametes to the embryo sac for fertilization.

Pollination may occur by abiotic agents such as wind and water, or by biotic agents such as insects, birds and animals. Based on the agent involved, important types of pollination are anemophily, hydrophily and entomophily.

General Mechanism of Pollination

During pollination, mature anthers dehisce and release pollen grains. These pollen grains are then transferred to the stigma by a suitable agent. If the pollen reaches a compatible stigma, it absorbs moisture and nutrients from the stigmatic surface and germinates. The intine comes out through the germ pore and forms a pollen tube. The pollen tube grows through the style and reaches the ovule, where fertilization occurs.

Thus, the basic sequence is:

Anther dehiscence → pollen release → transfer by agent → deposition on stigma → pollen germination → pollen tube formation
Type 1
02

Anemophily

Definition

Anemophily is the type of pollination in which pollen grains are transferred from anther to stigma by wind. Plants pollinated by wind are called anemophilous plants.

This type of pollination is common in grasses, cereals and many trees.

Examples: Maize, wheat, rice, sugarcane, grasses, coconut, date palm.

Mechanism of Anemophily

In anemophilous plants, flowers produce a large number of pollen grains. The anthers are usually exposed outside the flower and dehisce freely. The light, dry pollen grains are carried by air currents. Since wind movement is random, only a small number of pollen grains reach the stigma, while many are wasted.

To catch the wind-borne pollen, the stigma is usually large, branched or feathery. When pollen grains come in contact with the receptive stigma, they adhere to it and germinate.

In maize, the male flowers are present in the tassel at the top, while the female flowers are present in the cob. The long silky stigmas hang outside and catch pollen grains carried by wind.

Anemophilous flowers show special adaptations for wind pollination:

Type 2
03

Hydrophily

Definition

Hydrophily is the type of pollination in which pollen grains are transferred from anther to stigma through water. Plants pollinated by water are called hydrophilous plants.

Hydrophily is relatively rare and is found mainly in aquatic plants.

Examples: Vallisneria, Hydrilla, Zostera, Ceratophyllum.

Types of Hydrophily

Hydrophily may be of two types:

  1. Epihydrophily
  2. Hypohydrophily

A. Epihydrophily

In epihydrophily, pollination takes place on the surface of water.

Example: Vallisneria.

In Vallisneria, the female flower is borne on a long coiled stalk and reaches the surface of water. The male flowers detach from the plant and float on the water surface. These floating male flowers are carried by water currents. When they come near the female flower, anthers release pollen grains which reach the stigma. After pollination, the stalk of the female flower coils again and brings the flower under water, where fruit development occurs.

B. Hypohydrophily

In hypohydrophily, pollination takes place below the surface of water.

Examples: Zostera, Hydrilla.

In such plants, pollen grains are released under water. They are carried by water currents and reach the submerged stigma. In Zostera, pollen grains are long and ribbon-like, which helps them float and move easily in water.

Adaptations for Hydrophily

Hydrophilous plants show the following adaptations:

AdaptationDescription
Aquatic habitPlants live in water.
Reduced floral partsFlowers are often small and simple.
Absence of showy petalsNo need to attract insects.
Absence of nectar and scentAnimal attraction is not required.
Pollen adapted to waterPollen may be long, thread-like or protected from wetting.
Mucilaginous coveringHelps pollen remain protected in water.
Stigma exposed to water currentHelps in catching pollen.
Male flowers may floatSeen in surface pollination, as in Vallisneria.
Special Features Hydrophily is much less common than wind or insect pollination. Many aquatic plants are not truly hydrophilous because their flowers may rise above water and get pollinated by wind or insects. True hydrophily is limited to a few aquatic plants.
Type 3
04

Entomophily

Definition

Entomophily is the type of pollination in which pollen grains are transferred from anther to stigma by insects. Plants pollinated by insects are called entomophilous plants.

This is one of the most common and effective types of pollination in angiosperms.

Examples: Rose, sunflower, Salvia, Hibiscus, pea, orchids, mustard.

Mechanism of Entomophily

Entomophilous flowers attract insects by bright colour, fragrance, nectar and edible pollen. Insects such as bees, butterflies, moths, beetles and flies visit flowers for nectar or pollen.

When an insect enters a flower, pollen grains stick to its body because the pollen grains are sticky, rough or spiny. When the same insect visits another flower of the same species, the pollen grains are transferred to the stigma. If the stigma is receptive and the pollen is compatible, germination occurs.

This method is more efficient than wind pollination because pollen is directly transferred from flower to flower with less wastage.

Adaptations for Entomophily

Entomophilous flowers show many adaptations for attracting insects and ensuring successful pollination:

Brightly coloured petalsAttract insects from a distance.

Special Mechanism in Salvia

Salvia shows a special lever mechanism for insect pollination. The flower is bilabiate and has two stamens. Each stamen has a connective forming a lever-like structure. When an insect enters the flower to suck nectar, it pushes the sterile end of the lever. This causes the fertile anther lobe to strike the back of the insect and deposit pollen.

When the insect visits another mature flower, its pollen-covered body touches the stigma and pollination occurs. This is an example of a specialized entomophilous mechanism.

Special Features Entomophily is less wasteful than anemophily because insects carry pollen directly from one flower to another. It also promotes cross-pollination and genetic variation. Many flowers and insects show co-evolution, where floral structure and insect behaviour are closely related.

Significance of Pollination

Pollination is essential because it brings male gametes close to the female reproductive structure. It is the first step leading to fertilization, seed formation and fruit formation. Cross-pollination, especially by insects and wind, increases genetic variation and helps plants adapt to changing environments.

Different pollination mechanisms show how flowers are adapted to their environment and pollinating agents.

Conclusion Pollination is the transfer of pollen grains from anther to stigma. In anemophily, wind acts as the pollinating agent, and flowers are small, dull, nectarless, with light pollen and feathery stigma. In hydrophily, water acts as the agent, and aquatic plants show special adaptations such as floating flowers or water-resistant pollen. In entomophily, insects carry pollen, and flowers are usually showy, scented, nectar-producing and have sticky pollen. Thus, pollination shows close adaptation between flowers and their pollinating agents and plays a vital role in fertilization and seed formation in angiosperms.
Double Fertilization
06

Double Fertilization

Introduction

Double fertilization is a unique reproductive process found in angiosperms. It involves two fusion events inside the embryo sac. One male gamete fuses with the egg cell to form the zygote, while the other male gamete fuses with the two polar nuclei or secondary nucleus to form the primary endosperm nucleus.

Thus, in angiosperms, fertilization occurs twice, so the process is called double fertilization. It was first discovered by Nawaschin in Lilium and Fritillaria.

Definition

Double fertilization is the process in which the two male gametes released by the pollen tube take part in two separate fusions inside the embryo sac:

  1. One male gamete fuses with the egg cell to form the diploid zygote.
  2. The other male gamete fuses with the two polar nuclei or secondary nucleus to form the triploid primary endosperm nucleus.

The first fusion is called syngamy, and the second fusion is called triple fusion.

Structures Involved in Double Fertilization

Double fertilization takes place inside the embryo sac, which is the female gametophyte of angiosperms. A typical mature embryo sac is 7-celled and 8-nucleate.

RegionStructure
Micropylar endEgg apparatus: one egg cell and two synergids
Central regionCentral cell with two polar nuclei or secondary nucleus
Chalazal endThree antipodal cells

The male gametophyte is represented by the pollen grain. It produces two male gametes, which are carried to the embryo sac through the pollen tube.

Mechanism of Double Fertilization

1. Pollination and Pollen Germination

Double fertilization begins after pollination. When a mature pollen grain lands on a suitable stigma, it absorbs moisture and nutrients from the stigmatic surface. The pollen grain germinates, and the intine comes out through the germ pore to form a pollen tube.

The pollen tube grows down through the style towards the ovary. It carries the male gametes towards the ovule.

2. Formation of Two Male Gametes

In many angiosperms, pollen is shed at the two-celled stage. It contains a vegetative cell and a generative cell. The generative cell divides mitotically to form two male gametes. This division may occur either inside the pollen grain or inside the pollen tube.

Thus, the pollen tube finally contains a vegetative nucleus and two male gametes. The vegetative nucleus controls the growth of the pollen tube, while the male gametes take part in fertilization.

3. Entry of Pollen Tube into Ovule

The pollen tube enters the ovule commonly through the micropyle. This type of entry is called porogamy and is the most common method.

In some plants, the pollen tube may enter through the chalaza, called chalazogamy, or through the integuments, called mesogamy.

4. Entry into Embryo Sac

After entering the ovule, the pollen tube usually reaches the embryo sac through one of the synergids.

The pollen tube enters the embryo sac at the micropylar end, usually through a degenerating synergid. The synergids help in guiding the pollen tube because they possess a special structure called the filiform apparatus.

The filiform apparatus increases the surface area of the synergid and helps in absorption, secretion and pollen tube attraction. After reaching the embryo sac, the tip of the pollen tube bursts and releases the two male gametes.

Two Events of Double Fertilization

1. Syngamy

The first male gamete fuses with the egg cell. This fusion is called syngamy or true fertilization.

The egg cell is haploid, and the male gamete is also haploid. Their fusion produces a diploid zygote.

Male gamete (n) + Egg cell (n) = Zygote (2n)

The zygote later develops into the embryo.

2. Triple Fusion

The second male gamete moves towards the central cell and fuses with the two polar nuclei or the secondary nucleus. Since three haploid nuclei are involved in this fusion, it is called triple fusion.

Male gamete (n) + Two polar nuclei (n + n) = Primary endosperm nucleus (3n)

The primary endosperm nucleus later develops into the endosperm. The endosperm provides nourishment to the developing embryo.

Why the Process is Called Double Fertilization

This process is called double fertilization because both male gametes participate in fertilization. One male gamete takes part in syngamy and forms the zygote, while the other male gamete takes part in triple fusion and forms the primary endosperm nucleus. Thus, two fertilization events occur in the same embryo sac.

Products of Double Fertilization

Double fertilization produces two important structures:

  1. Zygote: The zygote is formed by the fusion of one male gamete with the egg cell. It is diploid and develops into the embryo.
  2. Primary Endosperm Nucleus: The primary endosperm nucleus is formed by the fusion of the second male gamete with the polar nuclei or secondary nucleus. It is usually triploid and develops into endosperm. The endosperm is a nutritive tissue that provides food to the developing embryo.

Significance of Double Fertilization

Double fertilization is one of the most important features of angiosperms. Its significance is as follows:

  1. Unique feature of angiosperms: Double fertilization is characteristic of flowering plants and helps distinguish them from other plant groups.
  2. Formation of embryo: Syngamy produces the zygote, which develops into the embryo.
  3. Formation of endosperm: Triple fusion produces the primary endosperm nucleus, which develops into endosperm.
  4. Economy of food material: Endosperm develops only after fertilization. Therefore, nutritive tissue is not wasted if fertilization does not occur.
  5. Coordination between embryo and endosperm development: Since embryo and endosperm formation begin almost together, the developing embryo receives proper nourishment at the right time.
  6. Restoration of diploid chromosome number: Syngamy restores the diploid condition in the zygote.
  7. Genetic variation: Fusion of male and female gametes combines genetic material from two parents, producing variation in offspring.

Conclusion Double fertilization is a characteristic feature of angiosperms in which both male gametes take part in fusion. One male gamete fuses with the egg cell to form a diploid zygote through syngamy. The other male gamete fuses with the two polar nuclei or secondary nucleus to form the triploid primary endosperm nucleus through triple fusion. The zygote develops into the embryo, while the primary endosperm nucleus forms the endosperm. Thus, double fertilization ensures both embryo formation and nutritive tissue formation, making it one of the most important reproductive adaptations of angiosperms.
